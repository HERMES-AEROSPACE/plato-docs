2. Transport Properties

This chapter describes the computation of viscosity, thermal conductivity, diffusion coefficients, and electrical conductivity in PLATO. The theory follows the Chapman–Enskog (CE) method applied to the Boltzmann equation for multi-component mixtures, as detailed in [Magin_thesis] (Chapters 1 and 4) and [Munafo_thesis] (Chapter 2). The implementation resides in src/transport/ (18 source files).

2.1. The Chapman–Enskog Method

The CE method derives macroscopic transport equations (Navier–Stokes) from the microscopic Boltzmann equation by exploiting a separation of time scales: elastic collisions are frequent (fast), while inelastic and reactive collisions are rare (slow).

The procedure, following [Magin_thesis] (Secs. 1.2–1.4), proceeds as:

  1. Expand the velocity distribution function around the local Maxwellian:

    \[f_i = f_i^{(0)} (1 + \epsilon \, \phi_i + \ldots)\]

    where \(\epsilon\) plays the role of the Knudsen number.

  2. Zeroth order (\(\epsilon^0\)): The distribution is a Maxwellian at the local mass-averaged velocity and temperature. For a two-temperature plasma [Magin_thesis] (Eq. 1.39):

    \[f_i^{(0)} = n_i \left(\frac{m_i}{2\pi k_{\mathrm{B}} T_i}\right)^{3/2} \exp\!\left(-\frac{m_i C_i^2}{2 k_{\mathrm{B}} T_i}\right)\]

    where \(T_i = T_h\) for heavy particles and \(T_i = T_e\) for electrons, and \(\mathbf{C}_i = \mathbf{c}_i - \mathbf{v}\) is the peculiar velocity.

  3. First order (\(\epsilon^1\)): The perturbation \(\phi_i\) is decomposed into contributions driven by gradients of velocity, temperature, and composition [Munafo_thesis] (Eq. 2.32):

    \[\phi_i = -\boldsymbol{\phi}_i^{\eta} : \nabla\mathbf{v} - \boldsymbol{\phi}_i^{\lambda} \cdot \nabla \ln T - \sum_j \boldsymbol{\phi}_i^{D_j} \cdot \mathbf{d}_j\]

    Each unknown function satisfies a linear integral equation with the linearized Boltzmann collision operator.

  4. Solve the integral equations by expanding \(\phi_i\) in Laguerre–Sonine (associated Laguerre) polynomials and projecting onto a finite basis. This converts the integral equations into linear algebraic systems whose matrix elements involve collision integrals.

  5. Extract transport coefficients (viscosity, conductivity, diffusion) from the solutions of these linear systems.

The resulting transport coefficients are expressed as solutions of linear systems of dimension \(O(n_{\mathrm{comp}})\), where \(n_{\mathrm{comp}}\) is the number of chemical components. All systems in PLATO are solved by direct methods (Cholesky or LU), inherited from the EGLIB library [Ern1994]. No external LAPACK is required.

2.2. Collision Integrals

Collision integrals \(\bar{Q}^{(\ell,s)}_{ij}\) encode the microscopic details of binary interactions. They are Maxwellian-averaged transport cross-sections [Magin_thesis] (Eq. 3.1):

(2.1)\[\bar{Q}_{ij}^{(\ell,s)}(T) = \frac{4(\ell+1)}{(s+1)!\,[2\ell+1-(-1)^\ell]} \frac{1}{(k_{\mathrm{B}} T)^{s+2}} \int_0^\infty e^{-E/(k_{\mathrm{B}} T)} \, E^{s+1} \, Q_{ij}^{(\ell)}(E) \, dE\]

where \(E = \frac{1}{2} \mu_{ij} g^2\) is the relative kinetic energy, \(\mu_{ij} = m_i m_j / (m_i + m_j)\) is the reduced mass, and \(Q_{ij}^{(\ell)}\) is the transport cross-section of order \(\ell\):

\[Q_{ij}^{(\ell)}(E) = 2\pi \int_0^\infty \left[1 - \cos^\ell \chi(E, b)\right] \, b \, db\]

with \(\chi(E, b)\) the deflection angle for a collision at energy E and impact parameter b [Magin_thesis] (Eqs. 3.2–3.3).

Physically:

  • \(\bar{Q}^{(1,1)}\) governs diffusion (momentum transfer)

  • \(\bar{Q}^{(2,2)}\) governs viscosity (shear transfer)

  • Higher orders \(\bar{Q}^{(1,2)}, \bar{Q}^{(1,3)}\) appear in the thermal conductivity and thermal diffusion matrix elements

2.2.1. Collision Integral Ratios

Three dimensionless ratios appear frequently in the transport matrix elements [Munafo_thesis] (Eqs. 2.63, 2.68), [Magin_thesis] (Eqs. B.4):

(2.2)\[A_{ij}^* = \frac{\bar{Q}_{ij}^{(2,2)}}{\bar{Q}_{ij}^{(1,1)}}, \qquad B_{ij}^* = \frac{5 \bar{Q}_{ij}^{(1,2)} - 4 \bar{Q}_{ij}^{(1,3)}} {\bar{Q}_{ij}^{(1,1)}}, \qquad C_{ij}^* = \frac{\bar{Q}_{ij}^{(1,2)}}{\bar{Q}_{ij}^{(1,1)}}\]

For rigid spheres, \(A^* = 1\), \(B^* = 1\), \(C^* = 1\). For realistic potentials, these ratios vary with temperature and characterize the angular dependence of scattering.

2.3. Binary Diffusion Coefficients

The first-order CE binary diffusion coefficient [Munafo_thesis] (Eq. 2.64), [Magin_thesis] (Eq. B.3b):

(2.3)\[n D_{ij} = \frac{3}{16} \sqrt{\frac{2\pi k_{\mathrm{B}} T (m_i + m_j)} {m_i m_j}} \frac{1}{\bar{Q}_{ij}^{(1,1)}}\]

where n is the total number density. For electron–heavy interactions, \(T \to T_e\) and the reduced mass simplifies to \(\approx m_e\) [Magin_thesis] (Eq. B.6b).

Source: src/transport/transport_matrix.F90, lines 47–97.

2.4. Dynamic Viscosity

2.4.1. Species Viscosity

The first-order CE viscosity of a pure species [Munafo_thesis] (Eq. 2.59), [Magin_thesis] (Eq. B.5):

(2.4)\[\eta_i = \frac{5}{16} \frac{\sqrt{\pi m_i k_{\mathrm{B}} T_h}} {\bar{Q}_{ii}^{(2,2)}}\]

2.4.2. Mixture Viscosity

The mixture viscosity is obtained from a symmetric linear system of dimension \(n_h\) (heavy particles only) [Munafo_thesis] (Eq. 2.56):

(2.5)\[\sum_{j \in H} G_{ij}^{\eta} \, \alpha_j^{\eta} = X_i, \qquad \eta = \sum_{i \in H} \alpha_i^{\eta} \, X_i\]

The matrix elements (first-order Sonine) are [Munafo_thesis] (Eqs. 2.57–2.58), [Magin_thesis] (Eqs. C.6a–b):

Off-diagonal (\(i \neq j\)):

(2.6)\[G_{ij}^{\eta} = \frac{X_i X_j}{n D_{ij} (m_i + m_j)} \left(\tfrac{6}{5} A_{ij}^* - 2\right)\]

Diagonal:

(2.7)\[G_{ii}^{\eta} = \frac{X_i^2}{\eta_i} + \sum_{j \neq i} \frac{X_i X_j}{n D_{ij} (m_i + m_j)} \left(\tfrac{6}{5} \frac{m_j}{m_i} A_{ij}^* + 2\right)\]

The system is solved by Cholesky decomposition (solve_sym_linsys from src/numerics/).

Note

In the source code (src/transport/transport_matrix.F90, lines 134–166), the matrix elements are assembled using the factor \(0.6 A^* = \tfrac{3}{5} A^*\). The off-diagonal element is computed as fac*(-1 + 0.6*A*) which equals \((\tfrac{6}{5} A^* - 2)/2\) times the common factor — this is equivalent to (2.6) after accounting for the different grouping of constants.

2.5. Thermal Conductivity

PLATO computes three contributions to the total thermal conductivity:

(2.8)\[\lambda = \lambda_h^{\mathrm{tr}} + \lambda^{\mathrm{int}} + \lambda_e\]

2.5.1. Heavy Translational Conductivity

Computed from a symmetric \(n_h \times n_h\) system analogous to viscosity [Munafo_thesis] (Eq. 2.60):

(2.9)\[\sum_{j \in H} G_{ij}^{\lambda} \, \alpha_j^{\lambda} = X_i, \qquad \lambda_h^{\mathrm{tr}} = \sum_{i \in H} \alpha_i^{\lambda} \, X_i\]

The matrix elements (second-order Sonine) are [Munafo_thesis] (Eqs. 2.61–2.62), [Magin_thesis] (Eqs. C.6c–d):

Off-diagonal (\(i \neq j\)), defining \(\hat{m}_{ij} = m_i/(m_i + m_j)\):

(2.10)\[G_{ij}^{\lambda} = \frac{1}{25 \, k_{\mathrm{B}}} \frac{X_i X_j}{n D_{ij}} \hat{m}_{ij} \hat{m}_{ji} \left(16 A_{ij}^* + 12 B_{ij}^* - 55\right)\]

Diagonal:

(2.11)\[G_{ii}^{\lambda} = \frac{X_i^2}{\lambda_i^{\mathrm{tr}}} + \sum_{j \neq i} \frac{1}{25 \, k_{\mathrm{B}}} \frac{X_i X_j}{n D_{ij}} \left[30 \hat{m}_{ij}^2 + 16 A_{ij}^* \hat{m}_{ij} \hat{m}_{ji} + (25 - 12 B_{ij}^*) \hat{m}_{ji}^2\right]\]

where \(\lambda_i^{\mathrm{tr}} = \tfrac{15}{4} \eta_i R_i\) is the species translational conductivity.

Source: src/transport/transport_matrix.F90, lines 267–273.

2.5.2. Internal Conductivity (Eucken Correction)

The internal (rotational + vibrational + electronic) contribution uses the generalized Eucken approximation [Magin_thesis] (Eqs. 2.13a–c), [Munafo_thesis] (Eq. 3.114):

(2.12)\[\lambda^{\mathrm{int}} = \frac{n_d}{N_A} \sum_{i \in H_p} \frac{c_{v,i}^{\mathrm{int}} \, m_i \, X_i} {\displaystyle\sum_{j \in H} X_j / D_{ij}}\]

where \(c_{v,i}^{\mathrm{int}} = c_{v,i}^{\mathrm{rot}} + c_{v,i}^{\mathrm{vib}} + c_{v,i}^{\mathrm{el}}\) and the sum is over polyatomic species \(H_p\).

The physical picture: internal energy is transported by molecular diffusion rather than by thermal conduction. The Eucken correction converts the diffusion coefficient into an effective thermal conductivity for internal modes.

Source: src/transport/transport_prop_lam_int.F90.

2.5.3. Electron Thermal Conductivity

Third-order Sonine polynomial approximation [Magin_thesis] (Eq. 4.4b):

(2.13)\[\lambda_e = \frac{75}{8} k_{\mathrm{B}} \sqrt{\frac{2\pi R_u}{m_e}} \; \frac{X_e \sqrt{T_e}} {\Lambda_{ee}^{11} - (\Lambda_{ee}^{12})^2 / \Lambda_{ee}^{22}}\]

where \(\Lambda_{ee}^{pq}\) are the electron–electron transport system matrix elements defined in terms of collision integral combinations [Magin_thesis] (Eqs. C.5c, C.5d, C.5f).

Source: src/transport/transport_prop_lam_sig_e.F90, lines 81–84.

2.6. Stefan–Maxwell Diffusion Equations

The species diffusion velocities are obtained from the Stefan–Maxwell equations, which express the balance between driving forces and inter-species friction.

2.6.1. Neutral Mixture

For a neutral mixture [Munafo_thesis] (Eqs. 2.69–2.72):

(2.14)\[\sum_{j \in S} G_{ij}^{D} \, V_j^d = -\hat{d}_i, \qquad i \in S\]

with the mass conservation constraint \(\sum_j \rho_j V_j^d = 0\).

The driving force \(\hat{d}_i\) includes mole fraction gradients and pressure diffusion:

\[\hat{d}_i = \nabla X_i + (X_i - y_i) \nabla \ln p\]

The matrix elements are [Munafo_thesis] (Eqs. 2.71–2.72):

\[G_{ij}^{D} = -\frac{X_i X_j}{D_{ij}} \;\; (i \neq j), \qquad G_{ii}^{D} = \sum_{k \neq i} \frac{X_i X_k}{D_{ik}}\]

The matrix is singular (all row sums vanish), so it is regularized with a mass-conservation term [Munafo_thesis] (Eq. 2.73): \(\tilde{G}_{ij}^D = G_{ij}^D + \tilde{a} \rho_i \rho_j\). The regularized symmetric system is solved by Cholesky decomposition.

2.6.2. Ionized Mixture (Ambipolar Diffusion)

For ionized mixtures, the Stefan–Maxwell system is extended with an ambipolar electric field that enforces charge neutrality [Magin_thesis] (Eqs. 1.94–1.95). The system becomes \((n_{\mathrm{comp}} + 1) \times (n_{\mathrm{comp}} + 1)\):

(2.15)\[\sum_{j \in S} G_{ij}^{D} V_j^d + \kappa_i \, E_a = -d_i'\]

with the zero-current constraint as the additional equation.

The electric field coupling coefficients are:

\[\kappa_i = \frac{1}{k_{\mathrm{B}} T_h} \left(X_i q_i - y_i \sum_j X_j q_j\right)\]

where \(q_i\) is the charge of species i.

For electron rows, the driving force is scaled by \(T_h/T_e\) to account for the different electron temperature [Magin_thesis] (Eq. 1.90b).

This non-symmetric system is solved by LU decomposition with partial pivoting (Crout’s algorithm from src/numerics/).

Source: src/transport/transport_flux.F90, src/transport/transport_matrix.F90, lines 343–436.

2.6.3. Thermal Diffusion (Soret Effect)

In addition to composition and pressure-driven diffusion, PLATO includes thermal diffusion (Soret effect): species diffuse along temperature gradients. The thermal diffusion ratios \(k_{T,i}^h\) appear in the full diffusion driving force [Munafo_thesis] (Eq. 2.65):

\[k_{T,i}^h = \frac{5}{2} \sum_{j \in H} \Lambda_{ij}^{01} \alpha_j^{\lambda}\]

where \(\Lambda_{ij}^{01}\) are the off-diagonal thermal diffusion matrix elements [Munafo_thesis] (Eqs. 2.66–2.67), [Magin_thesis] (Eqs. C.6e–f):

\[\Lambda_{ij}^{01} = \frac{1}{25 k_{\mathrm{B}}} \frac{X_i X_j}{n D_{ij}} \frac{m_i}{m_i + m_j} (12 C_{ij}^* - 10) \qquad (i \neq j)\]

2.7. Electrical Conductivity

Second-order Sonine polynomial approximation (default) [Magin_thesis] (Eq. 4.11b):

(2.16)\[\sigma_e = \frac{4}{25} \frac{(X_e \, e)^2}{k_{\mathrm{B}}^2 \, T_e} \frac{1}{\Lambda_{ee}^{00} - (\Lambda_{ee}^{01})^2 / \Lambda_{ee}^{11}}\]

where the denominator is the Schur complement of the 2\(\times\)2 Sonine system.

A third-order approximation is also available, where a \(3 \times 3\) symmetric system of \(\Lambda_{ee}^{pq}\) is solved [Magin_thesis] (Sec. 4.3).

Source: src/transport/transport_prop_lam_sig_e.F90, lines 91–108.
