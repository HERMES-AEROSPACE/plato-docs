5. Equilibrium Composition

This chapter describes PLATO’s algorithms for computing the chemical equilibrium (LTE) composition at a given \((p, T)\) or \((\rho, T)\). Two solvers are available:

  • A Newton–Raphson solver in log-space with Schur-complement reduction, following [Bottin1999]. This is the default. An extended version applies Debye–Hückel corrections [Griem1962] for ionized plasmas.

  • A Gibbs Function Continuation (GFC) solver following the multiphase formulation of [ScogginsMagin2015]. This is opt-in, requires LAPACK, and is the appropriate choice for stiff mixtures, mixtures near phase transitions, and problems with generalized linear constraints.

The Newton solver is implemented in src/thermo/thermo_equilibrium_comp.F90 (standard) and src/thermo/thermo_equilibrium_comp_dh.F90 (Debye–Hückel corrected). The GFC solver is implemented in src/thermo/thermo_equil_gfc.F90 with supporting modules src/thermo/thermo_equil_gfc_simplex.F90 (Simplex LP initialization) and src/thermo/thermo_equil_gfc_linalg.F90 (LAPACK wrappers).

The equilibrium constant that drives the law of mass action is derived from partition functions via microreversibility (see Equilibrium Constant from Partition Functions) or from Gibbs free energy via the NASA polynomial path [Magin_thesis] (Eq. 2.3).

5.1. Problem Formulation

At local thermodynamic equilibrium, the composition is determined by three sets of constraints:

  1. Element conservation — the total abundance of each element is prescribed

  2. Normalization — mole (or mass) fractions sum to unity

  3. Law of mass action — the equilibrium constant relates species concentrations for each independent reaction

These are combined into a nonlinear system of \(n_{\mathrm{comp}}\) equations in \(n_{\mathrm{comp}}\) unknowns.

5.1.1. Element Conservation

For each element \(e = 1, \ldots, n_{\mathrm{elem}}\):

(5.1)\[\sum_{c=1}^{n_{\mathrm{comp}}} \nu_{ec} \, X_c = X_e^0\]

where \(\nu_{ec}\) is the stoichiometric coefficient (number of atoms of element \(e\) in component \(c\)), \(X_c\) is the mole fraction, and \(X_e^0\) is the prescribed element mole fraction.

5.1.2. Normalization

(5.2)\[\sum_{c=1}^{n_{\mathrm{comp}}} X_c = 1\]

5.1.3. Law of Mass Action

For each linearly independent reaction \(r = n_{\mathrm{elem}}+1, \ldots, n_{\mathrm{comp}}\):

(5.3)\[\sum_{c=1}^{n_{\mathrm{comp}}} \nu_{rc} \, \ln X_c = \ln K_{\mathrm{eq},r}(T) - \Delta\nu_r \ln\!\left(\frac{p}{p_0}\right)\]

where \(\Delta\nu_r = \sum_c \nu_{rc}\) is the net change in moles.

The equilibrium constant is computed from either path:

  • Partition functions (default):

    (5.4)\[\ln K_{\mathrm{eq},r} = \sum_c \nu_{rc} \left[\ln Q_c(T) + \ln m_c + \frac{e_{f,c}}{k_{\mathrm{B}} T}\right] + \Delta\nu_r \ln\!\left(\frac{k_{\mathrm{B}} T}{p}\right)\]

  • NASA Gibbs free energy (--enable-nasa_poly):

    (5.5)\[\ln K_{\mathrm{eq},r} = -\frac{\Delta G_r^0(T)}{R_u T}\]

5.2. Newton–Raphson Solver in Log-Space

Following [Bottin1999], the system is solved by Newton–Raphson iteration in the logarithms of the unknowns, which automatically enforces positivity of all mole fractions:

\[\mathbf{x}^{(k+1)} = \mathbf{x}^{(k)} + \delta\mathbf{x}, \qquad \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{x}^{(k)}) \, \delta\mathbf{x} = -\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}^{(k)})\]

where \(\mathbf{x} = (\ln X_1, \ldots, \ln X_{n_{\mathrm{comp}}})^T\).

The unknowns are clipped after each iteration to prevent overflow: \(\ln X_c \in [\ln 10^{-99}, \, 0]\).

5.2.1. Schur Complement Reduction

The Jacobian is partitioned into element and reaction blocks:

(5.6)\[\begin{split}\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{J}_{ee} & \mathbf{J}_{er} \\ \mathbf{J}_{re} & \mathbf{J}_{rr} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \delta\mathbf{x}_e \\ \delta\mathbf{x}_r \end{bmatrix} = - \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{f}_e \\ \mathbf{f}_r \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

where:

  • \(\mathbf{J}_{ee}\) is \((n_{\mathrm{elem}}+1) \times (n_{\mathrm{elem}}+1)\) — element/normalization block

  • \(\mathbf{J}_{rr}\) is \((n_{\mathrm{comp}} - n_{\mathrm{elem}}) \times (n_{\mathrm{comp}} - n_{\mathrm{elem}})\) — reaction block

The Schur complement is:

\[\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{J}_{ee} - \mathbf{J}_{er} \, \mathbf{J}_{rr}^{-1} \, \mathbf{J}_{re}\]

The solution proceeds:

  1. Invert \(\mathbf{J}_{rr}\) (small, diagonal-dominant)

  2. Form and solve the reduced system \(\mathbf{S} \, \delta\mathbf{x}_e = -\tilde{\mathbf{f}}_e\)

  3. Back-substitute for \(\delta\mathbf{x}_r\)

This exploits the block structure and reduces the effective system size to \((n_{\mathrm{elem}}+1)\), which is small (e.g., 3 for air: N, O, + normalization).

5.2.2. Convergence Criterion

Convergence is declared when:

(5.7)\[\log_{10} \left\| \sqrt{\frac{1}{n_{\mathrm{comp}}} \sum_c \left(\frac{X_c^{(k+1)} - X_c^{(k)}}{X_c^{(k+1)}}\right)^2} \right\| < -12\]

The maximum number of iterations is 100. For well-conditioned problems, convergence is typically achieved in 3–5 iterations.

5.3. Debye–Hückel Corrections

For ionized plasmas, the standard equilibrium solver is extended with Debye–Hückel corrections (thermo_equilibrium_comp_dh.F90) that account for:

5.3.1. Coulomb Chemical Potential

The right-hand side of the law of mass action is modified:

\[\text{rhs}_r^{\mathrm{DH}} = \text{rhs}_r^{\mathrm{std}} - \frac{1}{k_{\mathrm{B}} T} \sum_c \nu_{rc} \, \mu_c^{\mathrm{Coulomb}}\]

where \(\mu_c^{\mathrm{Coulomb}}\) is the Coulomb chemical potential from the Debye–Hückel theory.

5.3.2. Debye Screening

The Debye length:

\[\lambda_D = \sqrt{\frac{\varepsilon_0 k_{\mathrm{B}} T_e} {n_e e^2 + \sum_{c \in \mathrm{ions}} Z_c^2 n_c e^2}}\]

The effective number density in the DH-corrected equation of state differs from the ideal gas value.

5.3.3. Ionization Potential Lowering (Griem’s Criterion)

The number of electronic levels included in the partition function is truncated based on Griem’s criterion: only levels whose binding energy exceeds the Coulomb interaction energy are retained. This prevents divergence of the partition function at high temperatures.

The truncation is applied iteratively at each Newton step via update_nb_el_levels_DH(), since the Debye length depends on the composition which is being solved for.

5.3.4. Coulomb Pressure and Energy

The equation of state includes Coulomb corrections:

\[p = p_{\mathrm{ideal}} + p_{\mathrm{Coulomb}}, \qquad e = e_{\mathrm{ideal}} + e_{\mathrm{Coulomb}}\]

These corrections also affect the adiabatic exponents \(\Gamma_1, \Gamma_2, \Gamma_3\).

Note

The DH-corrected solver uses a stricter convergence threshold (\(10^{-14}\)) and additional clipping (\(\ln X > -228\)) for numerical stability with strongly ionized plasmas.

5.4. Gibbs Function Continuation (GFC) Solver

The GFC solver in PLATO implements the multiphase Gibbs function continuation algorithm of [ScogginsMagin2015], with two PLATO-specific extensions for robustness at low temperature (described in PLATO Extensions to GFC).

5.4.1. Motivation

The difficulty in solving the equilibrium system (5.1)–(5.3) by Newton–Raphson is that the system Jacobian can become near-singular when the equilibrium composition lies close to the boundary of the feasible region defined by the constraints, or when condensed phases appear and disappear. In such situations the Newton initial-guess proximity condition cannot be satisfied in practice, because the condition number of the Jacobian diverges as the matrix approaches singularity [ScogginsMagin2015].

GFC avoids this failure mode by reformulating the static equilibrium problem as an initial-value problem in a continuation parameter \(s \in [0, 1]\), which is integrated from a cheap, approximate starting composition to the true equilibrium. The integration is globally convergent for any well-posed set of constraints.

5.4.2. Element Potential Equations

The GFC derivation starts from the Lagrangian of the Gibbs free energy minimization problem subject to the linear constraints (5.1). Introducing Lagrange multipliers \(\lambda_e\) (one per element) and setting the gradient of the Lagrangian to zero yields the element potential equations [ScogginsMagin2015] (Eq. 15):

(5.8)\[N_c = \bar{N}_{p_c} \exp\!\left( -\tilde{g}_c + \sum_{e=1}^{n_{\mathrm{elem}}} \lambda_e \nu_{ec} \right), \qquad c = 1, \dots, n_{\mathrm{comp}}\]

where \(N_c\) is the number of moles of component \(c\), \(\bar{N}_{p_c}\) is the total moles in its phase, \(\tilde{g}_c(T,p)\) is the non-dimensional pure-component Gibbs function, and \(\lambda_e\) are the element potentials. Substituting (5.8) into the mass-balance constraints (5.1) yields a nonlinear system of size \(n_{\mathrm{elem}}\) (plus \(n_{\mathrm{phase}}\) phase-mole unknowns in the multiphase case).

5.4.3. Continuation Formulation

Rather than solving (5.8) and (5.1) directly by Newton’s method, GFC introduces an auxiliary Gibbs vector \(\hat{g}(s)\) linear in the continuation parameter [ScogginsMagin2015] (Eq. 23):

(5.9)\[\hat{g}(s) = \hat{g}(0) + s \, [\tilde{g} - \hat{g}(0)], \qquad s \in [0, 1],\]

where \(\hat{g}(0)\) is chosen such that an easily-computed starting composition \(N^{(0)}\) satisfies the equilibrium equations exactly at \(s = 0\). The true equilibrium is reached at \(s = 1\). Its value is obtained by numerically integrating the tangent system

(5.10)\[\hat{x}(1) = \int_0^1 \frac{d\hat{x}}{ds}\, ds + \hat{x}(0),\]

where \(\hat{x}(s)\) stacks the element potentials \(\lambda(s)\) and the log-phase-moles \(\ln \bar{N}(s)\).

The tangent \(d\hat{x}/ds\) is determined from the implicit relation \(dR/ds = 0\), where \(R\) is the residual of the equilibrium equations. Its evaluation requires solving two small least-squares problems derived from the element-potential Jacobian; their solutions’ residuals lie in a null space that guarantees the tangent can be computed stably (see [ScogginsMagin2015], Section 3.2).

The integration is globally convergent: the system matrix whose factorization determines \(d\hat{x}/ds\) is proven to be symmetric positive definite with strictly positive eigenvalues for any well-posed set of constraints ([ScogginsMagin2015], Appendix A). Unlike Newton’s method, there is no proximity condition on the initial guess.

5.4.4. Initialization: Simplex LP

The GFC starting composition \(N^{(0)}\) is constructed as a convex combination of two compositions obtained by linear programming, both implemented in thermo_equil_gfc_simplex.F90:

  • The Min-G composition,

    \[N^{\mathrm{Min\text{-}G}} = \arg\min_{N \in \mathcal{B}} \, N^\top \tilde{g},\]

    minimizes the sum of species Gibbs energies subject to the constraints. Its Simplex-algorithm solution approximates the dominant species, but has at most \(n_{\mathrm{elem}}\) nonzero entries.

  • The Max–Min composition,

    \[N^{\mathrm{Max\text{-}Min}} = \arg\max_{N \in \mathcal{B}} \, \min_c N_c,\]

    maximizes the smallest component mole count subject to the same constraints. All components are strictly positive.

PLATO uses the recommended mixing fraction \(\alpha = 0.01\) ([ScogginsMagin2015] Eq. 28):

(5.11)\[N^{(0)} = (1 - \alpha) \, N^{\mathrm{Min\text{-}G}} + \alpha \, N^{\mathrm{Max\text{-}Min}},\]

so that the Min-G estimate dominates while the Max–Min contribution regularizes the logarithm in the Gibbs functional.

5.4.5. Integration and Phase Handling

The continuation integral (5.10) is evaluated with an adaptive forward-Euler step in \(s\), with a Newton correction applied at each step to drive the residual below tolerance. Step sizes halve on Newton failure and double on success, capped at \(\Delta s = 1\).

Condensed phases are handled by the vapor-pressure test [ScogginsMagin2015] (Eq. 47): at \(s = 1\), every phase not currently in the equilibrium solution is tested for whether its inclusion would lower the Gibbs energy. If so, the phase is added via the redistribution procedure of [ScogginsMagin2015] (Eqs. 48–50), and the continuation is restarted. Single-phase gas mixtures — the common case in PLATO’s hypersonic applications — skip this test.

5.4.6. PLATO Extensions to GFC

PLATO’s GFC implementation differs from the reference [ScogginsMagin2015] algorithm (as implemented in the Mutation++ library) in two ways, both targeting numerical robustness at low temperature. Both are applied post-convergence inside unpack_mole_fractions and do not modify the continuation integration itself.

5.4.6.1. Log-space charge-neutrality projection

For ionized mixtures, global charge neutrality

(5.12)\[\sum_c Z_c N_c = 0\]

(where \(Z_c\) is the charge number of component \(c\)) enters the GFC algorithm as one of the linear mass-balance constraints, with the electron playing the role of a “charge element.” This works correctly in aggregate: the total free charge on ions matches the total electron count to the tolerance of the solver.

However, the constraint (5.12) is a single linear relation that admits infinitely many numerical splits between individual positive ions and the electron population. At low temperature (\(T \lesssim 1700\) K in air), where the ionization fraction is itself at the level of the deep-cold floor \(X_c \sim 10^{-50}\) or smaller, the pseudo-equilibrium path integration can converge to a state in which the electron mole fraction \(X_{e^-}\) and, say, the atomic-ion mole fraction \(X_{\mathrm{O}^+}\) differ by many orders of magnitude while (5.12) remains satisfied in aggregate (the imbalance is absorbed by other ions at the floor). Such states are numerically valid solutions of the GFC residual equations but are unphysical.

PLATO addresses this by applying a symmetric projection onto the charge-neutrality manifold in log-space after GFC convergence. The projection operates on \(\ln X_c\) rather than \(X_c\), which is numerically appropriate because the individual ionic populations span many orders of magnitude at low temperature. The projection redistributes the small ionic populations so that pairwise charge balance is restored to machine precision without perturbing the total element moles or the neutral-species mole fractions.

The projection is triggered only when the aggregate charge-balance residual exceeds \(10^{-2}\) of the electron population — well above any physically meaningful imbalance but below the level at which the imbalance begins to corrupt downstream derivative and transport calculations.

5.4.6.2. Post-convergence deep-cold floor

The pseudo-equilibrium path in GFC can carry component mole fractions to values below \(10^{-99}\), at which point they are indistinguishable from floating-point noise. PLATO applies the same symmetric floor used by the Newton solver,

\[X_c \geq \exp(\texttt{DLOG\_TOL2}) \approx 10^{-99}, \qquad \texttt{DLOG\_TOL2} = -228,\]

post-convergence inside unpack_mole_fractions. This mirrors the Newton behavior exactly and ensures that downstream consumers (Gibbs derivatives, transport coefficients) see the same numerical floor regardless of which solver computed the equilibrium.

5.4.7. Solver agreement

With the above extensions in place, Newton and GFC are verified against each other across all PLATO regression mixtures (air5, air11, ablation products, Mars gas) at temperatures from 200 K to 20 000 K. The two solvers produce bit-identical component mole fractions at every tested point, including the low-temperature ionized regime where the reference [ScogginsMagin2015] algorithm (without the charge-neutrality projection) produces imbalanced results.

This agreement is a strong internal consistency check: two independently derived numerical methods, starting from different initial compositions and following different paths to the solution, converge on the same answer to the last bit of double precision. It also establishes that the chemical derivatives described in the next chapter (Chemical Derivatives) can be computed from either solver’s equilibrium state without affecting the numerical result.

5.4.8. When to Use GFC

In single-phase hypersonic gas mixtures above a few thousand kelvin, Newton and GFC produce bit-identical results and Newton is faster. GFC should be selected when:

  • the mixture includes condensed phases (ablation products, pyrolysis gases, soot);

  • generalized linear constraints beyond mass balance are imposed (for example, constraints on total moles or on bond counts);

  • Newton fails to converge on a stiff mixture near a phase transition;

  • an ionized mixture at low temperature is being computed, where the charge-neutrality projection ensures physical symmetry between electron and ion populations.

GFC is selected with the --solver gfc flag on the plato_equil CLI, or programmatically through the method argument of the equilibrium interface.
