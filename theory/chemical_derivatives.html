6. Chemical Derivatives

Chemical derivatives are partial derivatives of equilibrium mole fractions \(X_c\) with respect to the thermodynamic state variables \((T, p, X_e, Y_e)\) that define the equilibrium problem. They quantify how an equilibrium mixture rearranges itself in response to an infinitesimal change in state, and appear throughout the modeling of transport in equilibrium plasmas — whenever a gradient in temperature, pressure, or elemental composition drives a species gradient that in turn drives diffusion.

The derivation in PLATO follows the analytical formulation of [Orsini2008]. The implementation resides in src/thermo/thermo_equilibrium_deriv.F90 and is solver-agnostic: it takes a converged equilibrium state \((p, T, X_c)\) as input and does not depend on which solver (Newton or GFC, see Equilibrium Composition) produced it.

Historically, chemical derivatives were computed by finite differences: evaluate the equilibrium composition at two or more perturbed states and form a difference quotient. This approach is computationally expensive (each derivative requires at least one additional equilibrium solve) and prone to numerical error from the cancellation between nearly-equal compositions. PLATO’s analytical formulation bypasses both costs.

6.1. Problem Formulation

When an equilibrium flow field exhibits gradients in temperature, pressure, or elemental composition, the resulting species gradients decompose into contributions from each state variable [Orsini2008] (Eq. 28):

(6.1)\[\nabla X_c = \sum_{e=1}^{n_{\mathrm{elem}}} \frac{\partial X_c}{\partial X_e} \nabla X_e + p \frac{\partial X_c}{\partial p} \nabla \ln p + T \frac{\partial X_c}{\partial T} \nabla \ln T.\]

PLATO computes each of the partial derivatives on the right-hand side analytically, at machine precision, from a single equilibrium state.

6.1.1. Notation and Component Partition

The derivation partitions the \(n_{\mathrm{comp}}\) components into

  • \(n_{\mathrm{elem}}\) independent components, indexed by \(p, q\): a basis from which every other component can be formed as a linear combination;

  • \(n_{\mathrm{react}} = n_{\mathrm{comp}} - n_{\mathrm{elem}}\) dependent components, indexed by \(r, s\): written as reaction products of the independent components.

For high-temperature air with \(\mathcal{S} = \{e^-, \mathrm{N}, \mathrm{O}, \mathrm{N}_2, \mathrm{O}_2, \mathrm{N}^+, \mathrm{O}^+\}\), a natural choice of independent components matches the elements: \(\mathcal{I} = \{e^-, \mathrm{N}, \mathrm{O}\}\). The remaining components are dependent.

The stoichiometric matrix \(\nu_{ec}\) (introduced in Equilibrium Composition) gives the number of atoms of element \(e\) in component \(c\); for the dependent components \(r\), the same matrix entries describe \(r\) as a reaction product of the independent set.

6.1.2. Law of Mass Action in Differential Form

For each dependent reaction \(r\), equilibrium mole fractions obey the law of mass action (5.3). Differentiating with respect to each of the independent variables \((T, p, X_e)\) and applying the van ‘t Hoff relation for \(\partial \ln K_r / \partial T\) yields three sets of equations, one per variable. These are combined with the chain-rule constraints obtained by differentiating the element-composition relation \(X_e = \sum_c \nu_{ec} X_c\) ([Orsini2008], Eq. 13) to produce linear systems for the derivatives of the independent components. The derivatives of the dependent components are then obtained by back-substitution into the differentiated mass-action equations.

6.2. The Shared Jacobian

All four PLATO derivative outputs — with respect to temperature, pressure, element molar fractions, and element mass fractions — share the same coefficient matrix. They differ only in their right-hand sides. Following [Orsini2008] (Eq. 21), the matrix is

(6.2)\[A_{ep} = \hat{a}_{pe} + \sum_r \hat{a}_{re} \, \frac{X_r}{X_p} \, \nu_{pr},\]

of dimension \(n_{\mathrm{elem}} \times n_{\mathrm{elem}}\) — the number of elements, not components, which is typically one to two orders of magnitude smaller than a naive component-level system.

The chemical coefficients \(\hat{a}_{ce}\) are

(6.3)\[\hat{a}_{ce} = a_{ce} + \eta_e, \qquad a_{ce} = \frac{\nu_{ce} - X_e \sum_f \nu_{cf}} {\sum_c \left(\sum_f \nu_{cf}\right) X_c},\]

with \(\eta_e\) a gauge freedom discussed below (Gauge Conventions).

In PLATO, the matrix \(A_{ep}\) is assembled once per equilibrium state and LU-factorized via LAPACK. All four derivative types then reuse the factorization, so that each additional derivative costs only a back-substitution — far less than even a single finite-difference equilibrium evaluation.

6.3. The Four Right-Hand Sides

Each derivative type has its own right-hand side. In thermo_equilibrium_deriv.F90, the assembly of \(A_{ep}\), its factorization, and the chain-rule back-substitution are extracted into shared helpers (assemble_eq_deriv_jacobian and solve_and_unpack). Only the RHS construction is derivative-type-specific.

6.3.1. Temperature Derivative

Differentiating the law of mass action with respect to \(T\) and using the van ‘t Hoff relation gives [Orsini2008] (Eq. 20):

(6.4)\[\sum_p A_{ep} \, \frac{\partial X_p}{\partial T} = -\frac{1}{T} \sum_r \hat{a}_{re} \, X_r \, \frac{\Delta h_r}{R_u T},\]

where \(\Delta h_r = h_r - \sum_p \nu_{pr} h_p\) is the enthalpy of reaction \(r\) per mole of product, and \(h_c\) are the pure-component enthalpies. Condensed components contribute zero because their \(\partial \ln X / \partial T\) from the law of mass action vanishes.

6.3.2. Pressure Derivative

Differentiating the law of mass action with respect to \(p\) gives [Orsini2008] (Eq. 19):

(6.5)\[\sum_p A_{ep} \, \frac{\partial X_p}{\partial p} = -\frac{1}{p} \sum_r \hat{a}_{re} \, X_r \, (\nu_r - 1/n_{\mathrm{elem}}),\]

where \(\nu_r = \sum_p \nu_{pr}\) is the net stoichiometric change for reaction \(r\). Condensed components contribute zero, mirroring their treatment in the temperature derivative.

6.3.3. Element Molar-Fraction Derivative

Differentiating the element-composition relation \(X_e = \sum_c \nu_{ec} X_c\) with respect to a different element molar fraction \(X_f\) and combining with the differentiated law of mass action gives [Orsini2008] (Eq. 18):

(6.6)\[\sum_p A_{ep} \, \frac{\partial X_p}{\partial X_f} = \delta_{ef} - X_e,\]

where \(\delta_{ef}\) is the Kronecker delta. This RHS is purely geometric — it does not depend on component enthalpies or Gibbs energies — and the resulting derivative matrix has shape \(n_{\mathrm{comp}} \times n_{\mathrm{elem}}\).

6.3.4. Element Mass-Fraction Derivative

The mass-fraction derivatives are obtained from the molar-fraction derivatives by chain rule [Orsini2008] (Eqs. 22–27):

(6.7)\[\frac{\partial X_c}{\partial Y_e} = \sum_f \frac{\partial X_c}{\partial X_f} \frac{\partial X_f}{\partial Y_e},\]

with

\[\frac{\partial X_e}{\partial Y_f} = (\delta_{ef} - X_e) \, \frac{X_e}{Y_e}.\]

In PLATO, this derivative is computed by forming the element-direct RHS rather than composing two derivatives after the fact. This choice is motivated by numerical conditioning and by the need to match the Mutation++ convention of returning an \(n_{\mathrm{comp}} \times n_{\mathrm{elem}}\) matrix whose last column is filled via sum-rule closure.

6.4. Gauge Conventions

The chemical-coefficient offset \(\eta_e\) in (6.3) is a gauge freedom: the final derivatives \(\partial X_c / \partial X_e\) are gauge-invariant provided the associated sum-rule closure is applied consistently downstream. PLATO exposes two gauges through two separate derivative outputs:

  • The Orsini symmetric gauge (dXdxelem). Uses

    \[\eta_e = -\frac{1}{n_{\mathrm{elem}} \, n_{\mathrm{comp}}} \sum_c a_{ce}\]

    ([Orsini2008], Section III.A). Produces derivatives that satisfy the symmetric constraint \(\sum_c \partial X_c / \partial X_e = 0\). This is the convention of the published Orsini paper and of the dominant equilibrium-chemistry literature.

  • The Mutation++ “last-element-absorbs” gauge (dXdYe). Uses \(\eta_e = 0\) and fills the last column of the \(n_{\mathrm{comp}} \times n_{\mathrm{elem}}\) output matrix via the sum rule \(\sum_e \partial X_c / \partial Y_e = 0\). Matches the Mutation++ public API and is the convention most commonly expected by downstream transport codes.

Both gauges represent the same underlying tensor and produce equivalent results when correctly consumed. PLATO returns both because different downstream consumers expect different conventions, and converting between them requires knowledge of the component–element incidence that a consumer may not wish to reimplement. Internal parity tests confirm that the two representations satisfy the expected chain-rule consistency to \(10^{-12}\).

6.5. Domain of Validity

Chemical derivatives are evaluated at a prescribed state \((p, T, X_c)\) that must itself be a valid equilibrium state. Two boundary conditions affect this.

6.5.1. Minimum Temperature

The equilibrium solvers silently clamp requested temperatures to \(T_{\min} = 300\) K in their internal composition routine. For derivatives, PLATO enforces this boundary explicitly: requests for any derivative at \(T < T_{\min}\) return zero, at every primitive entry point (analytical and finite-difference, all four derivative types).

This explicit guard prevents a silently incoherent result in which the composition is evaluated at \(T_{\min}\) by the equilibrium routine while the derivative scaling uses the requested \(T\). The guard returns cleanly; downstream consumers receive zero derivatives, not a hybrid value combining clamped composition with uncontrolled scaling.

6.5.2. NASA-9 Polynomial Extrapolation

The thermodynamic database used by PLATO (NASA-9 polynomials [McBride2002]) is calibrated on specific temperature intervals. Outside these intervals, the polynomials are extrapolated silently, and their derivatives likewise. This is a property of the database, not of the solvers, and is the same behavior exhibited by Mutation++, CEA, and STANJAN. Users requesting derivatives at extreme temperatures should verify their thermodynamic database coverage.

6.6. Verification

PLATO’s analytical chemical derivatives are verified against two independent references:

  1. Internal finite-difference cross-check. Every analytical derivative has a centered finite-difference counterpart computed through the same public API with method = FD. Agreement is enforced to \(10^{-6} \cdot X_{\max}\) on nuclear-element columns of the derivative matrix and to absolute tolerance on the electron column (whose scale can differ from the nuclear columns by several orders of magnitude at high temperature).

  2. External Orsini reference. The regression test test/Fortran/regression/orsini_2008.F90 reproduces the numerical example of [Orsini2008] (high-temperature air, \(p = 1\) atm, elemental fractions \((X_{e^-}, X_\mathrm{N}, X_\mathrm{O}) = (0, 0.8, 0.2)\)) at \(T = 7000\) K and \(T = 14000\) K. Reference values are hand-digitized from [Orsini2008] Figs. 2–6. Absolute tolerances of 0.02–0.15 reflect the precision floor of the digitization, not of the analytical method.

  3. Full-range visual sweep. A companion test (test/Fortran/regression/orsini_sweep.F90 + orsini_plot.py) reproduces [Orsini2008] Figs. 2–6 visually for both solvers over \(T = 200\) to \(20\,000\) K. Newton and GFC produce bit-identical derivative values at every temperature in the sweep.

Additional internal consistency tests — bit-identity between the default API and the explicit analytical method, sum-rule consistency at machine epsilon, chain-rule consistency between dXdYe and dXdxelem at \(10^{-12}\), and activation of both the charge-balance guard and the \(T_{\min}\) guard as regression checks — are committed to the test suite.

6.7. Public API

! Unified entry point for all four derivative types
call plato_get_eq_composition_derivatives(                       &
     p, T, Xi,                                                   &
     method,                                                     &
     dXdT     = dXdT,                                            &
     dXdP     = dXdP,                                            &
     dXdYe    = dXdYe,                                           &
     dXdxelem = dXdxelem                                         &
)

Arguments:

  • p — pressure [Pa]

  • T — temperature [K] (must satisfy \(T \geq T_{\min}\) for non-zero output)

  • Xi(nb_species) — species mole fractions at the equilibrium state

  • method — ANALYTICAL (default) or FD

  • dXdT(nb_species) — optional, temperature derivative

  • dXdP(nb_species) — optional, pressure derivative

  • dXdYe(nb_species, nb_elem) — optional, element mass-fraction derivative, Mutation++ gauge

  • dXdxelem(nb_species, nb_elem) — optional, element molar-fraction derivative, Orsini symmetric gauge

All four derivative outputs are optional; only those requested are computed. The Jacobian is assembled and factorized once regardless of how many derivatives are requested.
