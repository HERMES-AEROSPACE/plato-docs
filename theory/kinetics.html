3. Chemical Kinetics

This chapter describes how PLATO computes chemical source terms (mass production rates and energy exchange), the rate coefficient models, and the analytical Jacobian. The presentation follows [Munafo_thesis] (Chapter 2) for the state-to-state formulation, [Munafo_thesis] (Chapter 3) for the multi-temperature and coarse-grained models, and [MunafoPanesi2025] for the complete implementation. The code resides in src/kinetics/ (22 files).

3.1. Rate Coefficients

3.1.1. Forward Rate: Modified Arrhenius Law

All forward rate coefficients in PLATO use the modified Arrhenius form:

(3.1)\[k_f(T) = A \, T^n \exp\!\left(-\frac{E_a}{k_{\mathrm{B}} T}\right) = A \exp\!\left(n \ln T - \frac{\theta_a}{T}\right)\]

where:

  • \(A\) is the pre-exponential factor

  • \(n\) is the temperature exponent

  • \(\theta_a = E_a / k_{\mathrm{B}}\) is the activation temperature (K)

The three parameters \((A, n, \theta_a)\) are read from the kinetics database files in database/kinetics/.

3.1.2. Activation Temperature for Multi-Temperature Models

For dissociation reactions in the multi-temperature (Park) model, the activation temperature uses a geometric average:

(3.2)\[T_{T_v} = T_h^{1-\alpha} \, T_v^{\alpha}\]

where \(\alpha\) is the Park parameter (typically 0.3–0.5). This appears in reaction types Dh_cat_mt (ID 2).

The temperature derivative is:

\[\frac{\partial T_{T_v}}{\partial T_h} = (1-\alpha) \frac{T_{T_v}}{T_h}, \qquad \frac{\partial T_{T_v}}{\partial T_v} = \alpha \frac{T_{T_v}}{T_v}\]

Source: src/kinetics/kinetics_rate.F90.

3.1.3. Backward Rate: Microreversibility

Backward rate coefficients are never read from the database. They are always computed from the principle of detailed balance (microreversibility): at equilibrium, every microscopic process is exactly balanced by its reverse. This fundamental constraint relates forward and backward rates [Munafo_thesis] (Eqs. 2.80–2.81).

The macroscopic backward rate is:

(3.3)\[k_b(T) = \frac{k_f(T)}{K_{\mathrm{eq}}(T)}\]

Derivation from microreversibility. Consider a state-specific dissociation reaction \(\mathrm{AB}(i) + \mathrm{M} \to \mathrm{A} + \mathrm{B} + \mathrm{M}\). At the microscopic level, detailed balance requires [Munafo_thesis] (Eq. 2.80):

\[\frac{k_i^{\mathrm{rec}}}{k_i^{\mathrm{dis}}} = \frac{g_i \, Q_{\mathrm{AB}}^{\mathrm{tra}}} {g_{\mathrm{A}} \, Q_{\mathrm{A}}^{\mathrm{tra}} \cdot g_{\mathrm{B}} \, Q_{\mathrm{B}}^{\mathrm{tra}}} \exp\!\left(\frac{E_{\mathrm{A}} + E_{\mathrm{B}} - E_i}{k_{\mathrm{B}} T}\right)\]

Summing over all internal levels i with Boltzmann weights yields the macroscopic equilibrium constant [Munafo_thesis] (Eq. 3.48):

(3.4)\[K_{\mathrm{eq}}(T) = \frac{Q_{\mathrm{AB}}^{\mathrm{tra}} \, Q_{\mathrm{AB}}^{\mathrm{int}}} {Q_{\mathrm{A}}^{\mathrm{tra}} \, Q_{\mathrm{B}}^{\mathrm{tra}}} \exp\!\left(\frac{E_{\mathrm{A}} + E_{\mathrm{B}}}{k_{\mathrm{B}} T}\right)\]

For a general reaction, the equilibrium constant is computed as (see also Equilibrium Constant from Partition Functions):

\[\ln K_{\mathrm{eq}} = \sum_c \nu_c \left[\ln Q_c^{\mathrm{tra}} + \ln Q_c^{\mathrm{int}} + \ln m_c - \frac{e_{f,c}}{R_c T}\right] + \Delta\nu \ln\!\left(\frac{k_{\mathrm{B}} T}{p}\right)\]

Alternative: Gibbs free energy path. When NASA polynomials are enabled [Magin_thesis] (Eq. 2.3):

(3.5)\[K_{\mathrm{eq}} = \exp\!\left( -\frac{\Delta G^0(T)}{R_u T} \right)\]

Both paths yield the same equilibrium constant; see Example 7: Thermodynamic Potentials for numerical verification.

Note

Enforcing microreversibility ensures thermodynamic consistency: the system always relaxes toward the correct equilibrium. Using independently fitted forward and backward rates can violate the second law and produce spurious behavior.

Source: src/kinetics/kinetics_rate.F90.

3.3. Mass Production Rates

The mass production rate of species s is:

(3.6)\[\dot{\omega}_s = m_s \sum_{r=1}^{N_r} \left( \nu_{s,r}^{\prime\prime} - \nu_{s,r}^{\prime} \right) \left( k_{f,r} \prod_{i} n_i^{\nu_{i,r}^{\prime}} - k_{b,r} \prod_{j} n_j^{\nu_{j,r}^{\prime\prime}} \right)\]

where \(\nu^{\prime}\) and \(\nu^{\prime\prime}\) are the reactant and product stoichiometric coefficients, and \(m_s\) converts from number to mass rates.

For catalyzed reactions (types 1, 2, 6, 14) involving a third body M, the production term includes the total number density of collision partners:

\[\dot{n}_{\mathrm{prod}} = n_M \left( n_i \, k_f - k_b \, n_j \, n_k \right)\]

where \(n_M = \sum_s n_s\) (or a weighted sum with specified efficiencies).

3.3.1. Source Term Computation Pipeline

The source term evaluation in get_source() proceeds as follows:

  1. Convert partial densities to number densities: \(n_i = \rho_i / m_i\)

  2. Clamp temperatures (enforce positivity)

  3. Compute forward rate coefficients \(k_f\) via (3.1)

  4. Compute backward rate coefficients \(k_b\) via (3.3)

  5. Loop over 16 reaction categories, accumulate production terms

  6. Convert to mass rates: \(\dot{\omega}_s = m_s \, \dot{n}_s\)

  7. Add energy exchange terms (Energy Exchange Terms)

Output: omega(1:nb_sp) = mass production rates [kg/m3/s], omega(nb_sp+nb_dim+1:...) = energy exchange rates [W/m3].

Source: src/kinetics/kinetics_source_mass.F90.

3.4. Energy Exchange Terms

PLATO computes six energy transfer mechanisms that couple the different temperature equations.

3.4.1. Vibration–Translation (VT) Relaxation

The Landau–Teller model describes VT relaxation as exponential approach to translational equilibrium [Munafo_thesis] (Eqs. 3.40–3.41):

(3.7)\[\Omega_{\mathrm{VT}} = \sum_{m \in \mathrm{mol}} \rho_m \frac{e_m^{\mathrm{vib}}(T_h) - e_m^{\mathrm{vib}}(T_v)} {\tau_{\mathrm{VT},m}}\]

Note the sign convention: \(\Omega_{\mathrm{VT}} > 0\) when \(T_h > T_v\), meaning energy flows from translation to vibration.

The mixture-averaged relaxation time uses Millikan–White mixing:

\[p \, \tau_{\mathrm{VT},m} = \left(\sum_h \frac{X_h}{p \, \tau_{h\text{-}m}}\right)^{-1}\]

where each pair relaxation time \(\tau_{h\text{-}m}\) is given by the Millikan–White correlation [MillikanWhite1963]:

\[p \, \tau_{h\text{-}m} = \exp\!\left[ a \left(T^{-1/3} - b\right) - 18.42 \right] \quad [\mathrm{atm \cdot s}]\]

with \(a = 1.16 \times 10^{-3} \mu_{hm}^{1/2} \theta_{\mathrm{vib}}^{4/3}\) and \(b = 0.015 \mu_{hm}^{1/4}\). A Park high-temperature correction [Park1989] is added at \(T > 8000\) K to prevent unphysical divergence.

Source: src/kinetics/transfer_vt.F90.

3.4.2. Vibration–Electron (VE) Exchange

For electron-impact vibrational excitation (primarily N2–e-):

(3.8)\[\Omega_{\mathrm{VE}} = \sum_{m} \frac{\rho_m \left[ e_m^{\mathrm{vib}}(T_e) - e_m^{\mathrm{vib}}(T_v) \right]} {\tau_{\mathrm{VE},m}}\]

The relaxation time \(\tau_{\mathrm{VE}}\) is computed from piecewise polynomial fits in \(\log_{10} T_e\).

Source: src/kinetics/transfer_ve.F90.

3.4.3. Translation–Electron (TE) Elastic Exchange

Elastic collisions between electrons and heavy particles:

(3.9)\[\Omega_{\mathrm{TE}} = \frac{3}{2} k_{\mathrm{B}} \, n_e \left(T_h - T_e\right) \nu_{eh}\]

where \(\nu_{eh}\) is the electron–heavy particle collision frequency.

Source: src/kinetics/transfer_te.F90.

3.4.4. Chemistry–Vibration (CV) Coupling

The vibrational energy added or removed by chemical reactions:

(3.10)\[\Omega_{\mathrm{CV}} = \sum_{m \in \mathrm{mol}} \sum_r \dot{\omega}_{m,r} \, e_m^{\mathrm{vib}}(T_v)\]

This is evaluated after the chemical source terms are computed.

Source: src/kinetics/transfer_cv.F90.

3.4.5. Chemistry–Electronic (CEL) Coupling

The electronic energy carried by chemical reactions:

(3.11)\[\Omega_{\mathrm{CEL}} = \sum_s \sum_r \dot{\omega}_{s,r} \, e_s^{\mathrm{el}}(T_e)\]

Source: src/kinetics/transfer_cel.F90.

3.4.6. Free-Free Bremsstrahlung (FF)

Energy loss by electron Bremsstrahlung radiation (Kramers formula with Gaunt factor = 1):

(3.12)\[\Omega_{\mathrm{ff}} = C_{\mathrm{ff}} \left(\sum_{c \in \mathrm{ions}} Z_c \, n_c\right) n_e \sqrt{T_e}\]

where \(Z_c\) is the charge number and \(C_{\mathrm{ff}}\) is a constant from the Kramers cross-section.

Source: src/kinetics/transfer_rad_ff.F90.

3.5. Analytical Jacobian

PLATO provides the full analytical Jacobian \(\partial(\dot{\omega}, \Omega) / \partial(\rho_i, T_k)\) for implicit time integration. The Jacobian matrix has dimensions:

\[\mathbf{J} \in \mathbb{R}^{(n_s + n_{\mathrm{dim}} + n_T) \times (n_s + n_{\mathrm{dim}} + n_T)}\]

where \(n_s\) is the number of species, \(n_{\mathrm{dim}}\) the spatial dimension, and \(n_T\) the number of temperatures.

Column ordering:

  • Columns \(1\) to \(n_s\): partial densities \(\rho_i\)

  • Columns \(n_s+1\) to \(n_s+n_{\mathrm{dim}}\): spatial coordinates

  • Remaining columns: temperatures \(T_h, T_v, T_e, \ldots\)

Key derivative terms (example for catalyzed dissociation, type 1):

\[\frac{\partial \dot{n}}{\partial n_i} = n_M \, k_f, \qquad \frac{\partial \dot{n}}{\partial n_j} = -k_b \, n_M \, n_k, \qquad \frac{\partial \dot{n}}{\partial T} = \left(\frac{n}{T} + \frac{\theta_a}{T^2}\right) n_M \left( n_i \, k_f - k_b \, n_j \, n_k \right)\]

The chain rule relates partial-density derivatives to number-density derivatives:

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial \rho_i} = \frac{1}{m_i} \frac{\partial}{\partial n_i}\]

For the Park activation temperature (3.2), additional chain-rule terms propagate \(\partial T_{T_v}/\partial T_h\) and \(\partial T_{T_v}/\partial T_v\) into the Jacobian.

Energy transfer Jacobians (VT, VE, TE, CV, CEL, FF) are included in the same matrix.

A mass-only Jacobian variant (\(n_s \times n_s\)) is available for simplified time integration.

Source: src/kinetics/kinetics_source_jac.F90.

3.6. Coarse-Grained Kinetics

For coarse-grained (CG) models, rate coefficients come from pre-tabulated binary lookup tables with bicubic interpolation rather than Arrhenius expressions. The Jacobian is computed numerically by finite differences (not analytically). Tables reside in database/kinetics/{ng}g/.

Source: src/kinetics/kinetics_group.F90.
