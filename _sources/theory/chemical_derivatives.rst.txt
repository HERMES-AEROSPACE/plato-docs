====================
Chemical Derivatives
====================

Chemical derivatives are partial derivatives of equilibrium mole
fractions :math:`X_c` with respect to the thermodynamic state variables
:math:`(T, p, X_e, Y_e)` that define the equilibrium problem. They
quantify how an equilibrium mixture rearranges itself in response to
an infinitesimal change in state, and appear throughout the modeling
of transport in equilibrium plasmas — whenever a gradient in
temperature, pressure, or elemental composition drives a species
gradient that in turn drives diffusion.

The derivation in PLATO follows the analytical formulation of
[Orsini2008]_. The implementation resides in
``src/thermo/thermo_equilibrium_deriv.F90`` and is **solver-agnostic**:
it takes a converged equilibrium state :math:`(p, T, X_c)` as input
and does not depend on which solver (Newton or GFC, see
:ref:`theory_equilibrium`) produced it.

Historically, chemical derivatives were computed by **finite
differences**: evaluate the equilibrium composition at two or more
perturbed states and form a difference quotient. This approach is
computationally expensive (each derivative requires at least one
additional equilibrium solve) and prone to numerical error from the
cancellation between nearly-equal compositions. PLATO's analytical
formulation bypasses both costs.


Problem Formulation
===================

When an equilibrium flow field exhibits gradients in temperature,
pressure, or elemental composition, the resulting species gradients
decompose into contributions from each state variable [Orsini2008]_
(Eq. 28):

.. math::
   :label: eq_deriv_decomposition

   \nabla X_c = \sum_{e=1}^{n_{\mathrm{elem}}}
                \frac{\partial X_c}{\partial X_e} \nabla X_e
              + p \frac{\partial X_c}{\partial p} \nabla \ln p
              + T \frac{\partial X_c}{\partial T} \nabla \ln T.

PLATO computes each of the partial derivatives on the right-hand side
analytically, at machine precision, from a single equilibrium state.


Notation and Component Partition
--------------------------------

The derivation partitions the :math:`n_{\mathrm{comp}}` components into

- :math:`n_{\mathrm{elem}}` **independent** components, indexed by
  :math:`p, q`: a basis from which every other component can be formed
  as a linear combination;

- :math:`n_{\mathrm{react}} = n_{\mathrm{comp}} - n_{\mathrm{elem}}`
  **dependent** components, indexed by :math:`r, s`: written as
  reaction products of the independent components.

For high-temperature air with
:math:`\mathcal{S} = \{e^-, \mathrm{N}, \mathrm{O}, \mathrm{N}_2,
\mathrm{O}_2, \mathrm{N}^+, \mathrm{O}^+\}`, a natural choice of
independent components matches the elements:
:math:`\mathcal{I} = \{e^-, \mathrm{N}, \mathrm{O}\}`. The remaining
components are dependent.

The stoichiometric matrix :math:`\nu_{ec}` (introduced in
:ref:`theory_equilibrium`) gives the number of atoms of element
:math:`e` in component :math:`c`; for the dependent components
:math:`r`, the same matrix entries describe :math:`r` as a reaction
product of the independent set.


Law of Mass Action in Differential Form
---------------------------------------

For each dependent reaction :math:`r`, equilibrium mole fractions obey
the law of mass action :eq:`eq_mass_action`. Differentiating with
respect to each of the independent variables :math:`(T, p, X_e)` and
applying the van 't Hoff relation for
:math:`\partial \ln K_r / \partial T` yields three sets of equations,
one per variable. These are combined with the chain-rule constraints
obtained by differentiating the element-composition relation
:math:`X_e = \sum_c \nu_{ec} X_c` ([Orsini2008]_, Eq. 13) to produce
linear systems for the derivatives of the independent components. The
derivatives of the dependent components are then obtained by
back-substitution into the differentiated mass-action equations.


The Shared Jacobian
===================

All four PLATO derivative outputs — with respect to temperature,
pressure, element molar fractions, and element mass fractions — share
the **same coefficient matrix**. They differ only in their right-hand
sides. Following [Orsini2008]_ (Eq. 21), the matrix is

.. math::
   :label: eq_deriv_jacobian

   A_{ep} = \hat{a}_{pe}
          + \sum_r \hat{a}_{re} \, \frac{X_r}{X_p} \, \nu_{pr},

of dimension :math:`n_{\mathrm{elem}} \times n_{\mathrm{elem}}` — the
number of *elements*, not components, which is typically one to two
orders of magnitude smaller than a naive component-level system.

The chemical coefficients :math:`\hat{a}_{ce}` are

.. math::
   :label: eq_chem_coeffs

   \hat{a}_{ce} = a_{ce} + \eta_e,
   \qquad
   a_{ce} = \frac{\nu_{ce} - X_e \sum_f \nu_{cf}}
                 {\sum_c \left(\sum_f \nu_{cf}\right) X_c},

with :math:`\eta_e` a gauge freedom discussed below
(:ref:`deriv_gauge`).

In PLATO, the matrix :math:`A_{ep}` is assembled once per equilibrium
state and LU-factorized via LAPACK. All four derivative types then
reuse the factorization, so that each additional derivative costs only
a back-substitution — far less than even a single finite-difference
equilibrium evaluation.


The Four Right-Hand Sides
=========================

Each derivative type has its own right-hand side. In
``thermo_equilibrium_deriv.F90``, the assembly of :math:`A_{ep}`, its
factorization, and the chain-rule back-substitution are extracted into
shared helpers (``assemble_eq_deriv_jacobian`` and
``solve_and_unpack``). Only the RHS construction is
derivative-type-specific.


Temperature Derivative
----------------------

Differentiating the law of mass action with respect to :math:`T` and
using the van 't Hoff relation gives [Orsini2008]_ (Eq. 20):

.. math::
   :label: eq_rhs_T

   \sum_p A_{ep} \, \frac{\partial X_p}{\partial T}
     = -\frac{1}{T} \sum_r \hat{a}_{re} \, X_r \,
        \frac{\Delta h_r}{R_u T},

where :math:`\Delta h_r = h_r - \sum_p \nu_{pr} h_p` is the enthalpy
of reaction :math:`r` per mole of product, and :math:`h_c` are the
pure-component enthalpies. Condensed components contribute zero
because their :math:`\partial \ln X / \partial T` from the law of mass
action vanishes.


Pressure Derivative
-------------------

Differentiating the law of mass action with respect to :math:`p`
gives [Orsini2008]_ (Eq. 19):

.. math::
   :label: eq_rhs_p

   \sum_p A_{ep} \, \frac{\partial X_p}{\partial p}
     = -\frac{1}{p} \sum_r \hat{a}_{re} \, X_r \,
        (\nu_r - 1/n_{\mathrm{elem}}),

where :math:`\nu_r = \sum_p \nu_{pr}` is the net stoichiometric change
for reaction :math:`r`. Condensed components contribute zero, mirroring
their treatment in the temperature derivative.


Element Molar-Fraction Derivative
---------------------------------

Differentiating the element-composition relation
:math:`X_e = \sum_c \nu_{ec} X_c` with respect to a different element
molar fraction :math:`X_f` and combining with the differentiated law
of mass action gives [Orsini2008]_ (Eq. 18):

.. math::
   :label: eq_rhs_xelem

   \sum_p A_{ep} \, \frac{\partial X_p}{\partial X_f}
     = \delta_{ef} - X_e,

where :math:`\delta_{ef}` is the Kronecker delta. This RHS is purely
geometric — it does not depend on component enthalpies or Gibbs
energies — and the resulting derivative matrix has shape
:math:`n_{\mathrm{comp}} \times n_{\mathrm{elem}}`.


Element Mass-Fraction Derivative
--------------------------------

The mass-fraction derivatives are obtained from the molar-fraction
derivatives by chain rule [Orsini2008]_ (Eqs. 22--27):

.. math::
   :label: eq_rhs_yelem

   \frac{\partial X_c}{\partial Y_e}
     = \sum_f \frac{\partial X_c}{\partial X_f}
       \frac{\partial X_f}{\partial Y_e},

with

.. math::

   \frac{\partial X_e}{\partial Y_f}
     = (\delta_{ef} - X_e) \, \frac{X_e}{Y_e}.

In PLATO, this derivative is computed by forming the element-direct
RHS rather than composing two derivatives after the fact. This choice
is motivated by numerical conditioning and by the need to match the
Mutation++ convention of returning an
:math:`n_{\mathrm{comp}} \times n_{\mathrm{elem}}` matrix whose last
column is filled via sum-rule closure.


.. _deriv_gauge:

Gauge Conventions
=================

The chemical-coefficient offset :math:`\eta_e` in
:eq:`eq_chem_coeffs` is a gauge freedom: the final derivatives
:math:`\partial X_c / \partial X_e` are gauge-invariant *provided* the
associated sum-rule closure is applied consistently downstream. PLATO
exposes two gauges through two separate derivative outputs:

- The **Orsini symmetric gauge** (``dXdxelem``). Uses

  .. math::

     \eta_e = -\frac{1}{n_{\mathrm{elem}} \, n_{\mathrm{comp}}}
              \sum_c a_{ce}

  ([Orsini2008]_, Section III.A). Produces derivatives that satisfy
  the symmetric constraint
  :math:`\sum_c \partial X_c / \partial X_e = 0`. This is the
  convention of the published Orsini paper and of the dominant
  equilibrium-chemistry literature.

- The **Mutation++ "last-element-absorbs" gauge** (``dXdYe``). Uses
  :math:`\eta_e = 0` and fills the last column of the
  :math:`n_{\mathrm{comp}} \times n_{\mathrm{elem}}` output matrix
  via the sum rule :math:`\sum_e \partial X_c / \partial Y_e = 0`.
  Matches the Mutation++ public API and is the convention most
  commonly expected by downstream transport codes.

Both gauges represent the same underlying tensor and produce
equivalent results when correctly consumed. PLATO returns both because
different downstream consumers expect different conventions, and
converting between them requires knowledge of the component–element
incidence that a consumer may not wish to reimplement. Internal
parity tests confirm that the two representations satisfy the expected
chain-rule consistency to :math:`10^{-12}`.


Domain of Validity
==================

Chemical derivatives are evaluated at a prescribed state
:math:`(p, T, X_c)` that must itself be a valid equilibrium state.
Two boundary conditions affect this.


Minimum Temperature
-------------------

The equilibrium solvers silently clamp requested temperatures to
:math:`T_{\min} = 300` K in their internal composition routine. For
derivatives, PLATO enforces this boundary **explicitly**: requests for
any derivative at :math:`T < T_{\min}` return zero, at every primitive
entry point (analytical and finite-difference, all four derivative
types).

This explicit guard prevents a silently incoherent result in which the
composition is evaluated at :math:`T_{\min}` by the equilibrium routine
while the derivative scaling uses the requested :math:`T`. The guard
returns cleanly; downstream consumers receive zero derivatives, not a
hybrid value combining clamped composition with uncontrolled scaling.


NASA-9 Polynomial Extrapolation
-------------------------------

The thermodynamic database used by PLATO (NASA-9 polynomials
[McBride2002]_) is calibrated on specific temperature intervals.
Outside these intervals, the polynomials are extrapolated silently,
and their derivatives likewise. This is a property of the database,
not of the solvers, and is the same behavior exhibited by Mutation++,
CEA, and STANJAN. Users requesting derivatives at extreme temperatures
should verify their thermodynamic database coverage.


Verification
============

PLATO's analytical chemical derivatives are verified against two
independent references:

1. **Internal finite-difference cross-check.** Every analytical
   derivative has a centered finite-difference counterpart computed
   through the same public API with ``method = FD``. Agreement is
   enforced to :math:`10^{-6} \cdot X_{\max}` on nuclear-element
   columns of the derivative matrix and to absolute tolerance on the
   electron column (whose scale can differ from the nuclear columns
   by several orders of magnitude at high temperature).

2. **External Orsini reference.** The regression test
   ``test/Fortran/regression/orsini_2008.F90`` reproduces the
   numerical example of [Orsini2008]_ (high-temperature air,
   :math:`p = 1` atm, elemental fractions
   :math:`(X_{e^-}, X_\mathrm{N}, X_\mathrm{O}) = (0, 0.8, 0.2)`)
   at :math:`T = 7000` K and :math:`T = 14000` K. Reference values are
   hand-digitized from [Orsini2008]_ Figs. 2--6. Absolute tolerances
   of 0.02--0.15 reflect the precision floor of the digitization, not
   of the analytical method.

3. **Full-range visual sweep.** A companion test
   (``test/Fortran/regression/orsini_sweep.F90`` +
   ``orsini_plot.py``) reproduces [Orsini2008]_ Figs. 2--6 visually
   for both solvers over :math:`T = 200` to :math:`20\,000` K. Newton
   and GFC produce bit-identical derivative values at every
   temperature in the sweep.

Additional internal consistency tests — bit-identity between the
default API and the explicit analytical method, sum-rule consistency
at machine epsilon, chain-rule consistency between ``dXdYe`` and
``dXdxelem`` at :math:`10^{-12}`, and activation of both the
charge-balance guard and the :math:`T_{\min}` guard as regression
checks — are committed to the test suite.


Public API
==========

.. code-block:: fortran

   ! Unified entry point for all four derivative types
   call plato_get_eq_composition_derivatives(                       &
        p, T, Xi,                                                   &
        method,                                                     &
        dXdT     = dXdT,                                            &
        dXdP     = dXdP,                                            &
        dXdYe    = dXdYe,                                           &
        dXdxelem = dXdxelem                                         &
   )

**Arguments:**

- ``p`` — pressure [Pa]
- ``T`` — temperature [K] (must satisfy :math:`T \geq T_{\min}` for
  non-zero output)
- ``Xi(nb_species)`` — species mole fractions at the equilibrium
  state
- ``method`` — ``ANALYTICAL`` (default) or ``FD``
- ``dXdT(nb_species)`` — optional, temperature derivative
- ``dXdP(nb_species)`` — optional, pressure derivative
- ``dXdYe(nb_species, nb_elem)`` — optional, element mass-fraction
  derivative, Mutation++ gauge
- ``dXdxelem(nb_species, nb_elem)`` — optional, element molar-fraction
  derivative, Orsini symmetric gauge

All four derivative outputs are optional; only those requested are
computed. The Jacobian is assembled and factorized once regardless of
how many derivatives are requested.
