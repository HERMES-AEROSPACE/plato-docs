===========================================
Example 3: Pressure Effects on Equilibrium
===========================================

Physical Context
================

Le Chatelier's principle states that an increase in pressure shifts
equilibrium toward the side with fewer moles of gas. Dissociation reactions
increase the number of particles:

.. math::

   \mathrm{N_2} \rightleftharpoons 2\mathrm{N} \qquad (\Delta n = +1)

Therefore, **higher pressure suppresses dissociation** (shifting the
equilibrium to the left) and **lower pressure promotes dissociation**
(shifting it to the right). This is quantified by the pressure dependence
in the law of mass action (:eq:`eq_mass_action`):

.. math::

   K_p(T) = \frac{X_{\mathrm{N}}^2}{X_{\mathrm{N_2}}}
   \frac{p}{p_0}

At the same temperature, increasing :math:`p` requires a smaller
:math:`X_{\mathrm{N}}^2 / X_{\mathrm{N_2}}` ratio, meaning less
dissociation.

**Theory reference:** :ref:`theory_equilibrium`.


Input Files
===========

Three input files at different pressures. All use air-5 over 300--15000 K.

**Low pressure** (1000 Pa ~ 0.01 atm, representative of high altitude):

.. code-block:: text

   # equil_air5_lowP.in
   PATH     = /path/to/database
   MIXTURE  = air5
   REACTION = empty
   TRANSFER = empty
   P        = 1000.d0
   T_MIN    = 300.d0
   T_MAX    = 15000.d0
   DT       = 100.d0
   OUTPUT   = output_lowP

**Standard pressure** (101325 Pa = 1 atm):

.. code-block:: text

   # equil_air5_1atm.in
   PATH     = /path/to/database
   MIXTURE  = air5
   REACTION = empty
   TRANSFER = empty
   P        = 101325.d0
   T_MIN    = 300.d0
   T_MAX    = 15000.d0
   DT       = 100.d0
   OUTPUT   = output_1atm

**High pressure** (1013250 Pa = 10 atm):

.. code-block:: text

   # equil_air5_highP.in
   PATH     = /path/to/database
   MIXTURE  = air5
   REACTION = empty
   TRANSFER = empty
   P        = 1013250.d0
   T_MIN    = 300.d0
   T_MAX    = 15000.d0
   DT       = 100.d0
   OUTPUT   = output_highP


Running the Example
===================

.. code-block:: bash

   cd build
   ./apps/equil/plato_equil equil_air5_lowP.in
   ./apps/equil/plato_equil equil_air5_1atm.in
   ./apps/equil/plato_equil equil_air5_highP.in


Results Plot
============

.. figure:: figures/ex03_pressure_effects.png
   :width: 90%
   :align: center

   N\ :sub:`2` mole fraction vs. temperature for air-5 at three pressures.
   At 1000 Pa, dissociation onset is near 1500 K. At 1 atm, onset is near
   3000 K. At 10 atm, onset is delayed to ~3500 K. This demonstrates Le
   Chatelier's principle: higher pressure suppresses dissociation.


Physical Interpretation
=======================

The pressure effect is dramatic:

- At **1000 Pa** (high altitude / low density): N\ :sub:`2` is 50%
  dissociated by ~3500 K.
- At **1 atm** (sea level): 50% N\ :sub:`2` dissociation occurs near ~5500 K.
- At **10 atm** (stagnation region): 50% dissociation is delayed to ~6000 K.

This has direct implications for re-entry heating:

- Stagnation-point flows (high pressure) remain more molecular, and the
  energy partition shifts toward translational modes.
- Wake regions (low pressure) dissociate extensively, storing energy in
  chemical form that can be released downstream (recombination heating).

The shift in crossover temperature with pressure follows from the equilibrium
constant:

.. math::

   T_{50\%} \propto \frac{D_0}{k_{\mathrm{B}} \ln(p/p_0) + \text{const.}}

where :math:`D_0` is the bond dissociation energy. The logarithmic dependence
on pressure means that each order-of-magnitude increase in :math:`p` shifts
the dissociation onset by a roughly constant temperature increment.

The plot is generated by ``scripts/plot_ex03.py``.
