Physical Modeling and Theory

Note

This page provides a summary of PLATO’s physical modeling framework. For the comprehensive mathematical derivations, see the Theory Guide, which covers:

  • Thermodynamic Properties — partition functions, energy, enthalpy, entropy, Gibbs and Helmholtz free energies, NASA polynomials

  • Transport Properties — Chapman–Enskog theory, collision integrals, viscosity, conductivity, diffusion

  • Chemical Kinetics — Arrhenius rates, microreversibility, source terms, energy exchange, analytical Jacobians

  • Nonequilibrium Models — MT, CG, CR, StS model hierarchy and polymorphic dispatch

  • Equilibrium Composition — Newton solver, Schur complement, Debye–Hückel corrections

For worked examples with input files and plots, see the Examples Guide.

This chapter presents the theoretical framework underlying PLATO: the governing equations for nonequilibrium multicomponent gas and plasma flows, the thermodynamic and transport closures, the chemical kinetics models, and the LTE simplification. Unless otherwise stated, the material follows the reference paper [MunafoPanesi2025]:

Munafò & Panesi, “PLATO: a Library for the Thermodynamics, Transport, and Kinetics of Multicomponent Gases and Plasmas,” Journal of Thermophysics and Heat Transfer, Vol. 39, No. 4, 2025. DOI: 10.2514/1.T7097

Introduction

PLATO implements the nonequilibrium kinetic theory for dilute gases, following the Chapman–Enskog (CE) method applied to the Boltzmann equation [Chapman1970], [Ferziger1972]. The key modeling assumptions are:

  1. Semiclassical framework — classical translational energy, quantum internal energy (rotation, vibration, electronic excitation).

  2. Collision-dominated regime — the gas is close enough to a local Maxwellian that the first-order CE expansion (Navier–Stokes level) is valid.

  3. Quasi-neutral plasma — charge separation occurs only at the Debye scale; the plasma is electrically neutral at the continuum scale.

  4. Unmagnetized plasma — the electron Hall parameter is small, so the magnetic field does not significantly modify transport coefficients.

  5. Maxwellian reaction regime — transport properties are unaffected by inelastic collisions, except for resonant charge transfer which enters the collision integrals.

Within this framework, PLATO provides a hierarchy of physicochemical models of increasing complexity:

  • Multitemperature (MT) models — Park-style [Park1989], correlation-based.

  • Coarse-grained (CG) reduced-order models — grouping of internal levels into bins.

  • Collisional-radiative (CR) models — resolved electronic levels.

  • State-to-state (StS) models — fully resolved rovibronic levels.

All models share the same governing equation structure; they differ in the definition of what constitutes a “species” and in how internal energy modes are described.

Species and Temperature Structure

Species Set

The species set is partitioned as

\[\mathcal{S} = \{e\} \cup \mathcal{H}\]

where \(e\) denotes free electrons and \(\mathcal{H}\) the heavy particles (atoms, molecules, and their ions). The heavy-particle set is further decomposed as \(\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{H}^n \cup \mathcal{H}^c\), with \(\mathcal{H}^n\) = neutrals and \(\mathcal{H}^c\) = charged heavy particles.

The meaning of “species” differs across model fidelity levels:

Model

Definition of species

MT

Chemical components: N2, O, NO+, etc.

CG

Grouped internal levels (bins): N2(g=1), N2(g=2), …

CR

Resolved electronic states: N(4S), N(2D), N(2P), …

StS

Fully resolved rovibronic levels: N2(v, J, el), …

This distinction is central to PLATO’s design: the same transport and kinetics algorithms operate on any species set, from 5-species air (MT) to thousands of pseudo-species (StS).

Temperature Structure

The gas is characterized by the following temperatures:

  • \(T_h\) — heavy-particle translational temperature.

  • \(T_e\) — free-electron translational temperature (distinct from \(T_h\) due to the large electron-heavy mass disparity).

  • \(\tilde{T}_1, \ldots, \tilde{T}_m\) — nonequilibrium internal-mode temperatures (vibrational, rotational, electronic), used in MT and CG models only.

The set \(\mathcal{I}\) denotes the internal degrees of freedom that are in thermal nonequilibrium with translation. In CR/StS models, \(\mathcal{I} = \emptyset\) because the populations of individual levels are tracked explicitly.

Two-temperature model (Park): The most common MT specialization sets \(T_e = \tilde{T}_v = \tilde{T}_{el} \equiv T_{ve}\) (vibronic temperature), so that a single additional equation (for the combined vibro-electronic energy) replaces the separate internal-mode and electron-energy equations.

Governing Equations

NLTE System

The full nonequilibrium system comprises the following conservation equations.

Species continuity (\(s \in \mathcal{S}\)):

(1)\[\frac{\partial \rho_s}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \bigl[\rho_s (\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{U}_s)\bigr] = \omega_s\]

where \(\rho_s\) is the species partial density, \(\mathbf{v}\) is the mass-averaged velocity, \(\mathbf{U}_s\) is the species diffusion velocity, and \(\omega_s\) is the mass production rate.

Global momentum:

(2)\[\frac{\partial (\rho \mathbf{v})}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v} \otimes \mathbf{v} + p\,\mathbf{I}) = \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau} + \mathbf{J} \times \mathbf{B}\]

where \(p\) is the total pressure, \(\boldsymbol{\tau}\) is the viscous stress tensor, \(\mathbf{J}\) is the current density, and \(\mathbf{B}\) is the magnetic field.

Global energy:

(3)\[\frac{\partial (\rho E)}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho H \mathbf{v}) = \nabla \cdot (\boldsymbol{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{v} - \mathbf{q}) + \Omega + \mathbf{J} \cdot \mathbf{E}\]

where \(E = e + \tfrac{1}{2}|\mathbf{v}|^2\) is the total energy per unit mass, \(H = h + \tfrac{1}{2}|\mathbf{v}|^2\) is the total enthalpy, \(\mathbf{q}\) is the total heat flux, and \(\Omega\) is the volumetric energy source (e.g., radiation).

Nonequilibrium internal energy (for each mode \(i \in \mathcal{I}\)):

(4)\[\frac{\partial (\rho e_i)}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho e_i \mathbf{v}) = -\nabla \cdot \mathbf{q}_i + \Omega_i\]

where \(e_i\) is the internal energy per unit mass for mode \(i\), \(\mathbf{q}_i\) is the internal-mode heat flux, and \(\Omega_i\) is the energy exchange source term for that mode.

Free-electron energy:

(5)\[\frac{\partial (\rho_e \varepsilon_e)}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho_e \varepsilon_e \mathbf{v}) = -\nabla \cdot \mathbf{q}_e - p_e\,\nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} + \Omega_e + \mathbf{J}_e \cdot \mathbf{E}'\]

where \(\varepsilon_e = \tfrac{3}{2}(k_B/m_e)T_e\) is the electron translational energy per unit mass, \(p_e = n_e k_B T_e\) is the electron pressure, \(\Omega_e\) accounts for energy exchange with heavy particles, and \(\mathbf{E}' = \mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}\) is the electric field in the hydrodynamic frame.

LTE System

Under local thermodynamic equilibrium (LTE), all temperatures are equal (\(T_h = T_e = \tilde{T}_i \equiv T\)), the composition is determined by the law of mass action, and the species and internal energy equations are replaced by algebraic relations. The governing equations reduce to:

Global mass:

(6)\[\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v}) = 0\]

Global momentum:

(7)\[\frac{\partial (\rho \mathbf{v})}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v} \otimes \mathbf{v} + p\,\mathbf{I}) = \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}\]

Global energy:

(8)\[\frac{\partial (\rho E)}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho H \mathbf{v}) = \nabla \cdot (\boldsymbol{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{v} - \mathbf{q})\]

Thermodynamics

Equation of State

The mixture is an ideal gas obeying Dalton’s law:

(9)\[p = p_h + p_e = n_h k_B T_h + n_e k_B T_e\]

where \(n_h = \sum_{s \in \mathcal{H}} n_s\) is the total heavy-particle number density and \(n_e\) is the free-electron number density. For a single-temperature gas (\(T_h = T_e = T\)), this simplifies to \(p = n k_B T\).

The total number density in terms of pressure and temperatures:

(10)\[n = \frac{p}{k_B T_h \bigl[1 + x_e (T_e / T_h - 1)\bigr]}\]

where \(x_e = n_e / n\) is the electron mole fraction.

Energy Content

The total internal energy per unit mass is decomposed as:

(11)\[e = \sum_{s \in \mathcal{H}} y_s \bigl[e^{tr}_s(T_h) + e^*_s(T_h, \tilde{T}_1, \ldots)\bigr] + \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} y_s\, e^f_s + \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} e_i + \varepsilon_e\]

where \(y_s = \rho_s / \rho\) is the mass fraction, \(e^{tr}_s\) is the translational energy, \(e^*_s\) collects the internal mode energies, \(e^f_s\) is the formation energy at the reference temperature, and \(\varepsilon_e\) is the free-electron translational energy.

Total energy and enthalpy:

\[E = e + \tfrac{1}{2}|\mathbf{v}|^2, \qquad H = h + \tfrac{1}{2}|\mathbf{v}|^2, \qquad h = e + p/\rho\]

Internal Energy Models

PLATO provides two thermodynamic paths, selected at configure time.

Path 1: Partition-function-based (default)

The species internal energy is computed from statistical mechanics using the partition function \(Z_s(T)\):

\[e^{int}_s(T) = \frac{k_B T^2}{m_s} \frac{\partial \ln Z_s}{\partial T}\]

The partition function is built from the following contributions:

Translational energy (3 degrees of freedom):

(12)\[e^{tr}_s(T) = \frac{3}{2}\frac{k_B}{m_s}\,T\]

Rigid-rotor rotational energy (linear molecules):

(13)\[e^{rot}_s(T_r) = \frac{k_B}{m_s}\,T_r\]

Harmonic-oscillator vibrational energy:

(14)\[e^{vib}_s(T_v) = \frac{k_B}{m_s} \frac{\theta^v_s}{\exp(\theta^v_s / T_v) - 1}\]

where \(\theta^v_s\) is the characteristic vibrational temperature.

Electronic energy (Boltzmann-weighted sum over levels):

(15)\[e^{el}_s(T_{el}) = \frac{1}{m_s} \frac{\sum_k g_k\, \epsilon_k\, \exp(-\epsilon_k / k_B T_{el})} {\sum_k g_k\, \exp(-\epsilon_k / k_B T_{el})}\]

where \(g_k\) and \(\epsilon_k\) are the degeneracy and energy of electronic level \(k\).

Formation energy:

\[e^f_s = \Delta h^0_{f,s}(T_{ref}) - \frac{k_B T_{ref}}{m_s}\]

where \(\Delta h^0_{f,s}\) is the standard enthalpy of formation at the reference temperature \(T_{ref} = 298.15\) K.

Path 2: NASA 9-coefficient polynomials (--enable-nasa_poly / -DPLATO_ENABLE_NASA_POLY=ON)

Thermodynamic properties are computed from piecewise polynomial fits:

\[\frac{C_p(T)}{R_s} = a_1 T^{-2} + a_2 T^{-1} + a_3 + a_4\,T + a_5\,T^2 + a_6\,T^3 + a_7\,T^4\]

with analogous expressions for enthalpy \(h(T)\) and entropy \(s(T)\) (including integration constants \(a_8\), \(a_9\)). This path provides entropy and Gibbs free energy, which are not available from the partition-function path.

LTE Secondary Properties

At chemical equilibrium, secondary thermodynamic properties are obtained from the equation of state and composition derivatives. Because the composition itself depends on \(T\) and \(p\), these derivatives involve the response of the equilibrium solver.

Specific heats:

\[C_p = T\left(\frac{\partial s}{\partial T}\right)_p, \qquad C_v = C_p - \frac{\alpha^2 T}{\rho\,\beta_T}\]

Coefficient of thermal expansion and isothermal compressibility:

\[\alpha = -\frac{1}{\rho}\left(\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial T}\right)_p = \frac{1}{T} + \frac{1}{R}\left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial T}\right)_p\]
\[\beta_T = \frac{1}{\rho}\left(\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial p}\right)_T = \frac{1}{p} - \frac{1}{R}\left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial p}\right)_T\]

where \(R = k_B / \bar{m}\) is the mixture-specific gas constant.

Equilibrium speed of sound:

(16)\[c_{eq} = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma_{eq}}{\rho\,\beta_T}}, \qquad \gamma_{eq} = \frac{C_p}{C_v}\]

Generalized adiabatic exponents [Mihalas1984]:

(17)\[\Gamma_1 = \left(\frac{\partial \ln p}{\partial \ln \rho}\right)_s = c_{eq}^2 \frac{\rho}{p}\]
(18)\[\frac{\Gamma_2}{\Gamma_2 - 1} = \left(\frac{\partial \ln p}{\partial \ln T}\right)_s = \frac{C_p\,\rho}{\alpha\,p}\]
(19)\[\Gamma_3 - 1 = \left(\frac{\partial \ln T}{\partial \ln \rho}\right)_s = \frac{\alpha}{\rho\,C_v\,\beta_T}\]

These reduce to \(\Gamma_1 = \Gamma_2 = \Gamma_3 = \gamma\) for a calorically perfect gas.

Equilibrium Composition

The LTE composition is computed by solving a nonlinear system combining:

  1. Element conservation (atom-balance constraints)

  2. Normalization (\(\sum X_s = 1\))

  3. Law of mass action (equilibrium constants \(K_{eq}\) from partition function ratios or NASA Gibbs free energy)

PLATO uses a Newton–Raphson solver in log-space following [Bottin1999], with Schur-complement reduction. Convergence criterion: \(\log_{10}(\|r\|_2) < -12\). A Debye–Huckel corrected variant is available for ionized plasmas (ionization potential lowering, Coulomb pressure correction, partition function truncation via Griem’s criterion).

Transport Properties

All transport properties are derived from the first-order Chapman–Enskog solution to the Boltzmann equation [Magin2004]. The resulting expressions involve collision integrals (see Collision Integrals) and are solved as linear systems of the form \(\mathbf{G}\,\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{b}\), where \(\mathbf{G}\) depends on species mole fractions and collision integrals.

Viscosity

Newton’s law for the viscous stress tensor:

(20)\[\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mu\bigl(\nabla\mathbf{v} + \nabla\mathbf{v}^T - \tfrac{2}{3}\,\mathbf{I}\,\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v}\bigr)\]

The shear viscosity \(\mu\) is computed from the heavy-particle subsystem at 1st Sonine order:

\[\mu = \mathbf{z}^\mu \cdot \mathbf{x}_h, \qquad \mathbf{G}^\mu\,\mathbf{z}^\mu = \mathbf{x}_h\]

where \(\mathbf{x}_h\) is the vector of heavy-particle mole fractions and \(\mathbf{G}^\mu\) is a symmetric positive-definite \(n_h \times n_h\) matrix depending on \(\bar{Q}^{(l,r)}_{sp}\) collision integrals. The system is solved by Cholesky (LDLT) decomposition.

Heat Flux

The total heat flux has three contributions:

(21)\[\mathbf{q} = \underbrace{-(\lambda^{tr}_h + \lambda^*_h)\nabla T_h + \sum_{s \in \mathcal{H}} \rho_s\,\mathbf{U}_s\,h_s}_{\text{heavy}} + \underbrace{\sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} \mathbf{q}_i}_{\text{internal modes}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{q}_e}_{\text{electron}}\]

Internal-mode heat flux (for \(i \in \mathcal{I}\)):

\[\mathbf{q}_i = -\lambda^i_h\,\nabla\tilde{T}_i + \sum_{s \in \mathcal{H}} \rho_s\,\mathbf{U}_s\,e^i_s\]

Free-electron heat flux:

\[\mathbf{q}_e = -\lambda^{tr}_e\,\nabla T_e + \rho_e\,\mathbf{U}_e\,h^{tr}_e + n k_B T_h \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \kappa^e_{T,s}\,\mathbf{U}_s\]

where \(\kappa^e_{T,s}\) are the electron thermal diffusion ratios.

Thermal Conductivity

Heavy translational \(\lambda^{tr}_h\) (1st Sonine):

\[\lambda^{tr}_h = \mathbf{z}^\lambda \cdot \mathbf{x}_h, \qquad \mathbf{G}^\lambda\,\mathbf{z}^\lambda = \mathbf{x}_h\]

Symmetric \(n_h \times n_h\) system, solved by Cholesky.

Internal modes \(\lambda^*_h\) (generalized Eucken correction):

\[\lambda^*_h = \sum_{s \in \mathcal{H}} \frac{x_s\,C^{int}_{v,s}\,\mu_s} {m_s}\]

where \(C^{int}_{v,s}\) is the internal specific heat at constant volume and \(\mu_s\) is the pure-species viscosity.

Electron translational \(\lambda^{tr}_e\) (3rd Sonine — higher order needed for accuracy):

(22)\[\lambda^{tr}_e = \frac{75}{64}\,k_B\, \frac{x_e^2 \sqrt{2\pi k_B T_e / m_e}} {\Lambda^{11}_{ee} - (\Lambda^{12}_{ee})^2 / \Lambda^{22}_{ee}}\]

where \(\Lambda^{ij}_{ee}\) are 2nd and 3rd Sonine expansion coefficients involving electron-heavy collision integrals. The expression is evaluated analytically (2x2 matrix inversion).

Reactive thermal conductivity (LTE only):

(23)\[\lambda^{rea} = -\sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \rho_s\,\tilde{U}_s\, h_s(T;\, T_{ref})\]

where \(\tilde{U}_s\) are the equilibrium diffusion velocities driven by \(\nabla T\) alone (computed by solving the Stefan–Maxwell system with the chemical potential gradient as driving force). This can be the dominant contribution to thermal conductivity in dissociating/ionizing regimes.

Diffusion Velocities (Stefan–Maxwell)

The species diffusion velocities \(\mathbf{U}_s\) are obtained from the Stefan–Maxwell equations. For heavy particles \(s \in \mathcal{H}\):

(24)\[\sum_{p \in \mathcal{S}} G^U_{sp}\,\mathbf{U}_p - \kappa_s\,\mathbf{E}' = -\mathbf{d}'_s\]

For electrons:

(25)\[\sum_{p \in \mathcal{S}} G^U_{ep}\,\mathbf{U}_p - \frac{T_h}{T_e}\,\kappa_e\,\mathbf{E}' = -\frac{T_h}{T_e}\,\mathbf{d}'_e\]

The modified driving forces include pressure, concentration, and thermal diffusion:

\[\mathbf{d}'_s = \frac{1}{n k_B T_h} \bigl[\nabla(n_s k_B T_s) - y_s\,p\,\nabla\ln p\bigr] + \frac{\kappa^h_{T,s}}{T_h}\,\nabla T_h + \frac{\kappa^e_{T,s}\,T_s}{T_e^2}\,\nabla T_e\]

Two constraints close the system: (i) mass conservation \(\sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \rho_s\,\mathbf{U}_s = 0\), and (ii) ambipolar diffusion (no net current in the absence of an external field):

\[\sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \kappa_s\,\mathbf{U}_s = 0\]

For neutral mixtures, the Stefan–Maxwell matrix is symmetric and solved by Cholesky decomposition (\(n_c \times n_c\)). For ionized mixtures, the matrix is non-symmetric (\((n_c+1) \times (n_c+1)\), including the ambipolar constraint) and solved by LU factorization (Crout’s method).

Thermal Diffusion Ratios

The thermal diffusion ratios \(\kappa^h_{T,s}\) (heavy) and \(\kappa^e_{T,s}\) (electron) appear in the Stefan–Maxwell driving forces and in the heat flux. They are computed from the same collision integral data using auxiliary linear systems at 1st Sonine order.

Electrical Conductivity

Full expression (Ohm’s law):

\[\sigma = \sum_{s,p \in \mathcal{S}} n_s\,q_s\,D_{sp}\,\kappa_p\]

where \(D_{sp}\) are multicomponent diffusion coefficients and \(\kappa_s = n_s q_s / (n k_B T_s)\) are the reduced charges.

Electron approximation (usually sufficient):

(26)\[\sigma_e = \frac{3}{16}\,\frac{x_e^2\,q_e^2}{k_B T_e}\, \sqrt{\frac{2\pi k_B T_e}{m_e}}\, \frac{\Lambda^{11}_{ee}} {\Lambda^{00}_{ee}\,\Lambda^{11}_{ee} - (\Lambda^{01}_{ee})^2}\]

PLATO computes \(\sigma_e\) at 2nd Sonine order by default (analytic 2x2 inversion), with the option of 3rd-order (Cholesky 3x3).

Generalized Ohm’s law:

\[\mathbf{J} \approx \mathbf{J}_e \approx \sigma_e\!\left(\mathbf{E}' - \frac{\nabla p_e}{n_e\,q_e}\right)\]

Collision Integrals

All transport properties are ultimately built from collision integrals, which encode the microscopic physics of binary interactions.

Definition

The reduced collision integrals are defined as:

(27)\[\bar{Q}^{(l,r)}_{sp} = \frac{4(l+1)}{(r+1)!\,[2l + 1 - (-1)^l]} \int_0^\infty e^{-\gamma^2}\,\gamma^{2r+3}\,Q^l_{sp}(\gamma)\,d\gamma\]

where \(\gamma^2 = \tfrac{1}{2} m_{sp} g^2 / (k_B T_{sp})\) is the reduced collision energy, \(g\) is the relative velocity, \(m_{sp} = m_s m_p / (m_s + m_p)\) is the reduced mass, \(T_{sp}\) is the effective temperature for the \((s,p)\) pair, and \(Q^l_{sp}\) is the \(l\)-th order transport cross-section [Ferziger1972].

Note

The notation \(\bar{Q}^{(l,r)}_{sp} = \pi\sigma^2_{sp}\, \Omega^{(l,r)*}_{sp}\) absorbs the reference cross-section \(\pi\sigma^2_{sp}\) into the definition, following the convention used in PLATO and EGLIB. This differs from some references where \(\Omega^{(l,r)*}_{sp}\) is dimensionless.

Derived Ratios

The following ratios of collision integrals appear in the transport coefficient matrices:

\[A^*_{sp} = \frac{\bar{Q}^{(2,2)}_{sp}}{\bar{Q}^{(1,1)}_{sp}}, \qquad B^*_{sp} = \frac{5\bar{Q}^{(1,2)}_{sp} - 4\bar{Q}^{(1,3)}_{sp}} {\bar{Q}^{(1,1)}_{sp}}, \qquad C^*_{sp} = \frac{\bar{Q}^{(1,2)}_{sp}}{\bar{Q}^{(1,1)}_{sp}}\]

Binary Diffusion Coefficients

The binary diffusion coefficient for the \((s,p)\) pair:

(28)\[D_{sp} = \frac{3}{16\,n\,\bar{Q}^{(1,1)}_{sp}} \sqrt{\frac{2\pi k_B T_{sp}(m_s + m_p)}{m_s m_p}}\]

Rate Coefficients

Forward rates use the modified Arrhenius form:

(29)\[k_f(T) = A\,T^n\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{E_a}{k_B T}\right)\]

where \(A\), \(n\), \(E_a\) are read from the kinetics database file. The activation temperature \(c = E_a / k_B\) is stored directly.

MT dissociation: For reactions governed by the Park two-temperature model, the controlling temperature is the geometric mean:

\[T_{Tv} = T_h^{1 - \alpha}\,T_{ve}^{\alpha}\]

with \(\alpha\) typically between 0.3 and 0.7.

Microreversibility and Detailed Balance

Backward rates are never read from the database. They are always computed from detailed balance:

(30)\[k_b(T) = \frac{k_f(T)}{K_{eq}(T)}\]

The equilibrium constant \(K_{eq}\) is obtained from:

  • Partition function ratios (default path):

    \[K_{eq}(T) = \prod_s \left(\frac{Z_s(T)}{V}\right)^{\nu_s} \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta e^f}{k_B T}\right)\]

    where \(\nu_s\) are stoichiometric coefficients and \(\Delta e^f\) is the formation energy difference.

  • NASA Gibbs free energy (--enable-nasa_poly):

    \[\ln K_{eq}(T) = -\frac{\Delta G^0(T)}{R\,T}\]

For CR/StS models, the level-specific reverse rate follows directly:

\[k_{ji}(T_e) = k_{ij}(T_e)\,\frac{g_i}{g_j}\, \exp\!\left(\frac{e^f_j - e^f_i}{k_B T_e}\right)\]

where \(g_i\), \(g_j\) are level degeneracies and \(e^f_i\), \(e^f_j\) are level formation energies per particle.

Mass Production Terms

The source term pipeline in PLATO:

  1. Convert partial densities \(\rho_s \to n_s\).

  2. Clamp temperatures (enforce positivity).

  3. Compute forward rate coefficients \(k_f\) and backward rates \(k_b = k_f / K_{eq}\).

  4. Loop over reaction categories, accumulate:

    \[\dot{n}_s = \sum_r \nu_{s,r}\bigl(k_{f,r}\,\Pi_{\text{react}} - k_{b,r}\,\Pi_{\text{prod}}\bigr)\]

  5. Convert to mass production rates: \(\omega_s = m_s\,\dot{n}_s\).

Analytical Jacobians

PLATO provides the full analytical Jacobian:

\[\mathbf{J} = \frac{\partial(\boldsymbol{\omega},\,\boldsymbol{\Omega})} {\partial(\boldsymbol{\rho},\,\mathbf{T})}\]

including chain-rule contributions for the Park activation temperature \(T_{Tv}\) and all energy exchange mechanisms. The mass-only Jacobian (\(n_s \times n_s\)) is also available. For coarse-grained (CG) models, a numerical Jacobian (finite differences) is used due to the tabulated rate coefficients.

LTE Formulation

Under LTE, the governing equations reduce to the Euler/Navier–Stokes system (Eqs. (6)–(8)) with the following closures.

Simplified Transport

The viscous stress tensor retains Newton’s law (Eq. (20)), but the heat flux simplifies to:

\[\mathbf{q} = -\lambda\,\nabla T\]

with total thermal conductivity:

\[\lambda = \lambda^{tr} + \lambda^{int} + \lambda^{rea}\]

The reactive thermal conductivity \(\lambda^{rea}\) (Eq. (23)) is computed by solving the Stefan–Maxwell system with the chemical potential gradient as the sole driving force and projecting the resulting diffusion velocities onto \(\nabla T\). This term can dominate the total conductivity in temperature ranges where dissociation or ionization is active.

Equilibrium Composition Solver

The composition at each (\(p\), \(T\)) or (\(\rho\), \(T\)) point is obtained from:

  1. Element conservation: \(\sum_s \nu_{es}\,X_s = X_e^0\) for each element \(e\).

  2. Normalization: \(\sum_s X_s = 1\).

  3. Law of mass action: \(K_{eq,r}(T) = \prod_s X_s^{\nu_{sr}} \cdot (p / p_0)^{\Delta\nu_r}\).

The system is solved by Newton–Raphson iteration in the logarithms of the unknowns (ensuring positivity). A Schur complement reduction exploits the block structure of the Jacobian (element and species blocks). Convergence is typically achieved in 3–5 iterations for well-conditioned problems.
